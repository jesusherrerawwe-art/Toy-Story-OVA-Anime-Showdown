Act III — The Unity
Chapter Six

Until Next Time, Cowboys

Point of View: Woody & Tanjiro

The clock on Bonnie's nightstand read 1:47 PM.

One hour and thirteen minutes. That was what they had left. One hour and thirteen minutes until Dennis's mom pulled into the driveway for the second time today, until the backpack was filled and zipped and carried out the front door, until six action figures who had spent the most confusing, terrifying, beautiful weekend of their manufactured lives in Bonnie Anderson's bedroom were returned to a shelf in a house across town.

Woody knew the math. He'd been doing it all afternoon — subtracting minutes the way a miser counted coins, feeling each one leave his hands. One hour and thirteen minutes. Then one hour and twelve. Then eleven. Then ten. The numbers got smaller and the room got louder and the distance between now and gone shrank with every tick of a clock that had no idea what it was counting down to.

The room was alive. Not the frantic, territorial alive of Friday night, when six strangers had tumbled out of cardboard boxes and a cowboy had drawn a line on the carpet to keep the peace. Not the shattered, quiet alive of Saturday night, when a green-haired boy had proved they'd never had powers and a Saiyan had asked "Does it matter?" and the answer had been a silence so full it was almost a song. This was a different kind of alive — the alive of people who knew the clock was running and had decided, collectively and without discussion, to spend every remaining minute being together.

Luffy had raided the crayon box and was teaching Rex how to draw a Jolly Roger. Rex was terrible at it. The skull looked like a potato. The crossbones looked like breadsticks. Luffy declared it the greatest pirate flag in history and demanded it be mounted on the toy chest immediately.

Naruto was sitting with Jessie on the windowsill, their legs dangling over the edge, watching Mr. Sprinkles patrol the backyard fence below. Naruto was explaining the concept of shadow clones in increasingly elaborate detail. Jessie was asking questions that made him stop and think — "But if the clone has your memories, how does it know it's not the real one?" — and Naruto was discovering, to his visible delight, that someone was actually interested.

Ichigo and Buzz were at the desk, and the sight of them together would have been impossible to imagine forty-eight hours ago. Buzz was showing Ichigo the star map printed on the inside of his helmet — the constellations of a fictional galaxy, hand-painted by someone in a Pixar design studio who had taken the time to make each one unique. Ichigo studied them with the careful attention of someone who appreciated craftsmanship, and when Buzz pointed out the star called "Gamma Quadrant Sector Four," Ichigo said, quietly, "That one looks like the Soul Society." Buzz didn't ask what the Soul Society was. He just nodded and said, "Then that's what it is."

Deku was in the corner with Pricklepants, and they were doing what they always did: talking. But the conversation had changed. It wasn't analysis anymore, wasn't the frantic hypothesis-testing of a mind trying to make sense of the impossible. It was storytelling. Deku was telling Pricklepants about All Might — not the statistical All Might, not the power levels and the injury timeline and the quirk transfer mechanism, but the person. The way he smiled. The way he said "I am here" and meant it. The way his hand felt when it rested on your shoulder and you knew — you just knew — that everything was going to be all right.

Pricklepants: "He sounds rather like a character I once played in a production of The Velveteen Rabbit. The old skin horse. The one who explains what it means to become Real."

Deku: "...Yes. He was exactly like that."

And Tanjiro — Tanjiro was with Forky. Of course he was. They were sitting on the bed, and Forky was showing him something: a crack in his spork handle, a thin line that ran from the base of one googly eye down to the curve of his bowl. It had been there since a dishwasher incident three months ago. Bonnie had cried. Bonnie's mom had glued it back together with superglue and a steadiness that came from understanding that some things mattered more than their material cost.

Forky: "See? It's still there. The crack. The glue holds it but you can still see it. Bonnie says it gives me character. I don't know what character means exactly. Is it good?"

Tanjiro: "It's the best thing you can have." He touched the crack gently, tracing it with a fingertip that was barely larger than the line itself. "In my world — in the world I remember — there is an art called kintsugi. When something breaks, you repair it with gold. The cracks become the most beautiful part. Not because the breaking was good, but because the mending was done with love."

Forky: "The glue isn't gold though. It's superglue. It was ninety-nine cents."

Tanjiro: "The gold isn't about the material, Forky. It's about who holds you while the glue dries."

Forky stared at him. Then Forky stared at the crack. Then Forky whispered, with the gravity of someone who has just understood something enormous:

Forky: "Bonnie held me."

Tanjiro: "Then the crack is already gold."

🕐 ⭐ 🕐

At 2:15, Goku called a meeting.

Not a Woody meeting — not the kind with a roll call and a threat assessment and a carefully drawn escape route. A Goku meeting, which meant he stood on the desk and said "Hey, everybody come here" in a voice that somehow managed to be both casual and impossible to ignore.

They gathered. All eighteen of them. Around the desk, on the desk, leaning against the desk. The blue crayon line was directly below them, running across the carpet like a scar that had healed but never fully faded. Nobody looked at it. Nobody needed to.

Goku: "So. We're leaving soon. And where I come from — where I remember coming from — when you leave a place that mattered, you leave something behind. A mark. A promise. Something that says 'I was here and it changed me.'" He looked around the room — at the toys who had been strangers and were now something else, something that didn't have a word in any language he knew. "So we made you stuff."

Woody: "You made us — stuff?"

Naruto: "Gifts. We made gifts. While you guys were napping this afternoon. Don't make it weird."

Naruto went first. He pulled something from behind the pencil cup — a tiny headband, fashioned from a strip of blue fabric torn from the corner of an old sock and a small piece of aluminum foil smoothed into a rectangle and scratched with the symbol of the Hidden Leaf. It was crude. It was crooked. It was, unmistakably, a work of sincere effort.

He gave it to Jessie.

Naruto: "You asked me about shadow clones. Nobody ever asks me about shadow clones. Everyone just says 'stop doing that.' But you asked how. And why. And that —" He rubbed the back of his head, the way he did when his feelings were bigger than his words. "That means you're a ninja. Unofficial. Honorary. The first cowgirl ninja in any world."

Jessie: (holding the headband with both hands, eyes shining) "I'm gonna wear this every night. Every. Single. Night."

Luffy gave Rex the pirate flag — the potato-skull, breadstick-crossbone masterpiece, wrapped around a broken pencil and jammed into a ball of clay from Bonnie's art supplies. "You're an honorary member of the Straw Hat crew," Luffy announced. "Dinosaur division. You're the only member so far but that makes you the captain of it."

Rex cried. Actual, silent, no-tear crying, which was the only kind a plastic dinosaur could do and was somehow more devastating for it.

Ichigo gave Buzz a small square of paper — an index card from Deku's collection, folded once, with a symbol drawn on it in black marker. Clean, precise, unhurried. The symbol of the Gotei 13 — the thirteen divisions of the Soul Society.

Ichigo: "Squad 13. The one that protects. The one that goes where it's needed, not where it's safe." He handed it to Buzz without ceremony, without eye contact, without any of the usual Ichigo performance of not caring. "You'd have been captain material, Lightyear. If things were different."

Buzz: (taking the card, studying the symbol, then tucking it carefully inside his wrist communicator panel) "I'll keep it with my star map."

Deku gave Pricklepants his index cards — all of them. Every card he'd used to map the room, to chart the power experiments, to document the three-hypothesis proof. Every calculation. Every observation. Every note in that tiny, meticulous handwriting that fit more words per square inch than should have been physically possible.

Deku: "It's everything I learned here. Every observation. Every theory." He paused, and his voice dropped to the quiet register that only came out when the feelings were too large for the analytical mind to contain. "Including the most important one: that the data doesn't always tell you what matters. Sometimes you have to feel it."

Pricklepants: (accepting the cards as though receiving a manuscript from Shakespeare himself) "I shall guard these with my life. And possibly publish them."

And Tanjiro — Tanjiro reached into his haori, the checkered fabric that was part of his molding but had a tiny pocket where the paint hadn't fully sealed, and pulled out a small, dried geranium petal. Pink. Creased. Carrying the faintest ghost of a scent that had no business surviving a night in a planter box and a morning in a bedroom and all the chaos between.

He gave it to Woody.

Tanjiro: "From the garden. From last night. You came for me, and I was sitting with the stars and the flowers, and I want you to have this so that — so that you know —" His voice caught. Tanjiro's voice never caught. Tanjiro was the steady one, the gentle one, the one who bowed first and spoke softly and held the room together with a quiet strength that didn't need volume. But it caught now. "So that you know the stars were real. And the rescue was real. And everything between this moment and the first time you called us 'his people' — that was real too."

Woody took the petal. It was lighter than anything he'd ever held. It weighed more than anything he'd ever carried.

Woody: "...Thank you."

Two words. Not a speech. Not a plan. Not a law or a rule or an emergency protocol. Just two words, said by a cowboy holding a flower petal, standing over a blue crayon line that had once been the most important boundary in the room and was now just a mark on the carpet — a memory of a morning when they'd been strangers and hadn't yet learned what they were about to become.

🎁 ⭐ 🎁

The doorbell rang at 2:58 PM. Two minutes early.

The sound traveled up the stairs like a verdict — bright, cheerful, completely indifferent to the weight of what it meant. Doorbells didn't know about goodbyes. Doorbells just rang.

Bonnie's Mom: (from downstairs) "Bonnie! Dennis's mom is here for his toys!"

Eighteen toys froze. Not the practiced, deliberate freeze of objects hiding their sentience — the involuntary freeze of beings who had just heard the clock run out.

Bonnie's feet thundered up the stairs. The door opened. A seven-year-old with paint-stained fingers and a juice box stain on her shirt walked in, looking for the figures Dennis had left behind. She didn't know what she was interrupting. She never would.

She found them exactly where they'd been "left" — arranged neatly on the desk, in the positions Woody had suggested twenty minutes ago. Goku in front. Naruto to his right. Luffy to his left. Ichigo slightly behind, arms crossed. Deku with his notebook hand extended. Tanjiro at the edge, turned slightly toward the bed, as if looking at the room one last time.

Bonnie scooped them up — all six — in a single gentle armful. She carried them downstairs the way she carried everything she cared about: close to her chest, with both arms, with the unconscious tenderness of a child who didn't yet know that the world would eventually ask her to carry things less carefully.

At the front door, Dennis was waiting. His backpack was open. His eyes were the specific kind of wide that only happened when you were seven and your favorite things were being returned.

Dennis: "Are they okay? Did they get hurt? Luffy was on the counter —"

Bonnie: "They're fine. I took really good care of them. My toys were nice to them."

She handed them over one by one. Goku. Naruto. Luffy. Ichigo. Deku. And last — held for one extra second, turned in her hands like she was memorizing the weight of him — Tanjiro.

Bonnie: "I really liked this one. He's... kind. You can tell, you know? Even when they're just toys, you can tell."

Dennis took Tanjiro carefully. Placed him in the backpack with the others. Zipped it shut. And then he said the thing that children say when they don't know they're saying something extraordinary:

Dennis: "Can we do this again? Like... next weekend?"

Dennis's Mom: "We'll see, sweetie. Say thank you to Bonnie's mom."

Dennis: "Thank you!" He turned to Bonnie, backpack clutched to his chest. "And tell Woody my guys said thanks too."

He said it casually. The way children said extraordinary things — without weight, without gravity, without any understanding of the worlds they were bridging with a single sentence.

The front door closed. The car started. The sound of the engine faded down the street and was replaced by the particular silence that only existed in a house after someone had left it — the silence that was shaped like the people who were no longer there.

🚪 ⭐ 🚪

Bonnie went back to her nature documentary. The house settled. And upstairs, in a bedroom that was suddenly larger than it had been ten minutes ago, twelve toys stood in the quiet and felt the specific weight of a room that had held eighteen.

Nobody spoke. Not because there was nothing to say, but because the nothing was important. The nothing needed space. The nothing was the shape of six figures who had been here — right here, on this desk, on that windowsill, under the bed, by the Lego house — and were now in a backpack in a car driving east on Maple Street toward a dentist-appointment-postponing mother's house, getting farther away with every second.

Jessie was holding the tiny headband. Turning it in her hands. The foil leaf symbol caught the afternoon light.

Rex was standing by the toy chest, the pirate flag planted beside him like a battle standard, his tiny arms at his sides, his expression the complicated mixture of pride and grief that only Rex could manage because his face was designed for one emotion and his heart insisted on feeling all of them.

Buzz had his wrist panel open. The Squad 13 card was visible inside, tucked between the painted circuits, as permanent as anything could be in a world made of plastic and intentions.

Woody was at the window. The geranium petal was in his vest pocket — the pocket that had carried nothing since the day he'd lost the photograph of Andy that he'd kept there for fifteen years until the ink faded and the paper dissolved and all that remained was the shape of the memory it had held. Now there was a petal there. Pink. Fragile. Real.

Jessie: "Do you think they'll be okay?"

Woody: "They'll be okay."

Jessie: "Do you think Dennis takes care of them? The way Bonnie takes care of us?"

Woody: "I think Dennis carries them in a backpack everywhere he goes and counts them before bed and introduces them to his stuffed animals by name. Yeah, Jessie. I think he takes care of them."

Hamm: "So what now? Back to normal?"

Woody: "Back to Bonnie's room. That's what we do. That's what we've always done."

Slinky: "It's gonna be quieter."

Dolly: "Dramatically. Blissfully. Tragically quieter."

Buzz: "Woody."

Woody turned from the window. Buzz was standing by the desk, looking at the carpet. At the blue line.

Buzz: "What do you want to do about the line?"

Woody looked at it. The blue crayon line. The one he'd drawn Friday night at 11:47 PM, on his hands and knees, with a Crayola Midnight Blue gripped in both hands, dragging it across the carpet with the focused precision of someone building a wall. It had been a border. Then a battleground. Then a negotiation table. Then a bridge. Then nothing. Then everything.

Now it was a line on a carpet in a room where six people had been and gone and left behind a headband, a pirate flag, a symbol, a stack of cards, and a single dried petal from a garden that a Demon Slayer had watched the stars from.

Woody: "Leave it."

Buzz: "Leave it?"

Woody: "Leave it. It's not a wall anymore, Buzz. It's a..." He searched for the word. The right word. The Woody word. "It's a bookmark. It marks the place where the story changed."

Forky: (from the bed, where he'd been sitting quietly for the first time in his entire existence) "I'm not a bookmark."

Woody: (the smallest smile) "No, Forky. You're not."

📖 ⭐ 📖

The backpack smelled like fruit snacks and pencil shavings.

Tanjiro had catalogued these scents in the first thirty seconds — a habit from a life he remembered but had never lived, the Demon Slayer's nose that his plastic body mimicked in instinct if not in function. The fruit snacks were grape. The pencil shavings were cedar. And underneath both, buried in the fabric like a secret, was the smell of Dennis: soap and grass and the particular sweetness of a child who still believed that action figures could hear him when he talked to them.

The car moved. The backpack swayed. And in the dark, pressed between Goku's elbow and Naruto's headband, Tanjiro felt the distance grow.

Naruto: (whispering, in the dark of the backpack) "You okay?"

Tanjiro: "I gave him the petal."

Naruto: "...Yeah. I saw."

Tanjiro: "It was the only real thing I had. The only thing that came from outside, from the garden, from a place that wasn't a box or a shelf or a room. It was proof that I'd been somewhere. That I'd seen something. And I gave it away." A pause. The car turned right. The backpack tilted. "I don't regret it. But I feel the space where it was."

Goku: "That's how you know it was worth giving."

Ichigo: "For once, I agree with the Saiyan."

Luffy: "I gave Rex a flag and I feel GREAT."

Ichigo: "That's because you have no emotional depth."

Luffy: "Thanks!"

Deku: "Do you think we'll go back?"

The question hung in the dark. Six figures, six different answers, six different versions of hope — some loud, some quiet, some wrapped in sarcasm, some raw and unprotected.

Goku: "Dennis asked for next weekend."

Naruto: "His mom said 'we'll see.' 'We'll see' is parent for 'probably.'"

Ichigo: "'We'll see' is parent for 'I'll think about it,' which is parent for 'maybe,' which is parent for 'stop asking.'"

Naruto: "You are the WORST at hope."

Ichigo: "I'm realistic."

Tanjiro: "You came back from the Soul Society."

Ichigo went quiet. In the dark, no one could see his face, but everyone could feel the shift — the crack in the armor that Ichigo wore like a second skin, the tiny fracture where something softer lived underneath.

Tanjiro: "You came back from the Soul Society. Naruto came back from failing the academy exam three times. Luffy survived Impel Down. Deku was born without a quirk and became a hero anyway. And Goku —" He turned toward Goku in the dark. "Goku has died. Twice. And come back both times."

Goku: (grinning in the dark, which was invisible but somehow audible) "Technically more than twice if you count the movies."

Tanjiro: "My point is — we are not people who don't go back. We are people who always go back. That is what we do. That is who we are. Whether or not we're the originals, whether or not we have powers, whether or not we're six inches tall and made of thermoplastic —" A word he'd learned from Ichigo, used now with the precision of someone who'd earned it. "We go back. That's our nature. And I choose to believe that nature doesn't change just because the body does."

The car stopped. Dennis's house. The engine cut off. Dennis's mom opened the back door and Dennis grabbed the backpack and the world lurched and shifted and the six figures tumbled against each other in the dark —

And then: light. Dennis's room. A shelf. A familiar shelf, with a familiar view of a familiar ceiling and a familiar window and the familiar sound of a boy dropping his backpack by the door and running to the bathroom because he'd held it the entire car ride.

Tanjiro stood on the shelf. His shelf. The one he'd stood on before the backpack and the playdate and the planter box and the stars. The same shelf, in the same room, in the same house. Nothing had changed.

Everything had changed.

He could still smell the geraniums. Faintly. In the place where the petal had been. A ghost of a scent that would fade in hours and be gone by morning and live in his memory forever — however memories worked for a toy, however "forever" worked for a being made of plastic and paint and whatever impossible, unnamed thing turned matter into feeling.

Through the window, the sky was turning amber. Late afternoon. The same sun that had warmed the porch where he'd sat with the geraniums. The same sun that had lit the kitchen floor during the rescue sprint. The same sun that was now, at this exact moment, shining through Bonnie's butterfly curtains and falling on a blue crayon line on a carpet in a room across town where a cowboy doll was standing at a window with a pink petal in his vest pocket.

The same sun. The same sky. Different windows.

For now.

🌅 ⭐ 🌅

That night, in two bedrooms in two houses on two sides of the same town, two toys stood at their windows and watched the dark.

In Bonnie's room, a cowboy stood on the windowsill with his hat tipped back and his pull-string resting against his spine and a geranium petal tucked into the pocket over his heart. Below him, the room was quiet. Jessie was wearing the headband. Rex had the pirate flag. Buzz had the Squad 13 card. The glow-in-the-dark stars on the ceiling were doing their slow, faithful fade, and Mr. Sprinkles was asleep on the hallway rug, his tail twitching in dreams of birds he would never catch.

In Dennis's room, a Demon Slayer stood on a shelf with his hand resting on the hilt of a sword that would never cut anything and his eyes turned toward a window that faced west — toward a house he couldn't see but knew was there, the way you know the sun is there even when it's on the other side of the world.

They couldn't hear each other. They couldn't see each other. They were separated by miles and walls and the small, enormous fact of being objects in a universe that didn't know they were alive.

But they were thinking the same thing. At the same time. In the same dark.

Woody-san told me something on the porch, when we were carrying Forky's words between us like a shared prayer. He said: "Being a toy isn't about what you can do. It's about who you do it for."

I've been a Demon Slayer. I've carried my sister through mountains. I've breathed fire and water and felt my blade sing in my hands. And none of it — not one moment of it — felt more real than sitting in a planter box under a sky full of stars, knowing that three toys were crossing a kitchen floor to bring me home.

We are small. We are plastic. We are six inches tall in a world measured in miles. And today, we were heroes — not because we had powers, but because we had each other. Because a cowboy taught a Saiyan how to sprint. Because a Space Ranger told a Soul Reaper he'd have made captain. Because a spork showed me that gold doesn't have to shine to be precious.

Dennis is asleep. His breathing is steady. It sounds — and I know this is impossible, I know this is just the echo of a memory I may never have lived — it sounds like Nezuko's.

Maybe that's enough. Maybe that's the answer to the question we've been asking since we opened our eyes in a cardboard box: What are we for?

We are for this. We are for the child who holds us. We are for the cracks that need gold. We are for the shelf and the backpack and the planter box and the stars. We are for each other.

Until next time. — Tanjiro
I've been Andy's toy. I've been Bonnie's toy. I've been a sheriff, a leader, a friend, a rival, a rescue coordinator, and — for one extraordinary weekend — a bridge between two worlds that should never have met and will never be the same for having tried.

Six warriors walked into a bedroom. They came with powers they couldn't use, memories they couldn't verify, and identities built on a foundation that turned out to be thermoplastic. And in forty-eight hours, they did something that I've watched toys struggle with for thirty years: they found out what they were made of. Not the plastic. Not the paint. The other stuff. The stuff that doesn't have a name and doesn't need one because you know it when you feel it and you feel it when someone crosses a kitchen floor for you and you feel it when someone gives you a flower petal and you feel it when a seven-year-old girl holds a Demon Slayer figure for one extra second and says, "He's kind — you can tell."

Yeah. You can tell.

The petal is in my pocket. The line is on the carpet. The stars are fading on the ceiling. And somewhere across town, on a shelf in a room I've never seen, a boy with a backpack is sleeping, and six figures are standing in the dark, and one of them — the gentle one, the one who bows first, the one who taught a spork about gold — is looking out a window that faces this direction.

I can't see him. I can't hear him. But I know he's there. The way you know anything that matters: not because you can prove it, but because the alternative — a world where he's not there, where they were never here, where this weekend was just a playdate and nothing more — is a world I refuse to live in.

Dennis said "next weekend." His mom said "we'll see." But I've been a toy long enough to know that when a child wants something badly enough, "we'll see" always — always — becomes "yes."

So I'll wait. I'll stand at this window and I'll wait. Because that's what toys do. We wait. We endure. We hold the space between the goodbyes and the hellos and we fill it with the quiet, stubborn, unreasonable certainty that the people we love are coming back.

And when they do — when that backpack opens and those boxes rattle and six voices fill this room again with arguments about ramen and sea kings and shadow clones and whether a dinosaur can learn martial arts —

I'll be right here. Hat on. Boots on. Pull-string ready.

Reach for the sky, partner. There's a friend in all of us. — Woody
