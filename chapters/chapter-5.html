Act III — The Unity
Chapter Five

Operation: Get Back

Point of View: Woody

Sunday morning arrived the way trouble always did in Woody's experience — with sunshine and the absolute certainty that something was about to go wrong.

The playdate was supposed to end at noon. Dennis's mother would arrive, collect her son and his backpack full of anime figures, and life would return to the comfortable, predictable rhythm of Bonnie's room. That was the plan. Woody had counted the hours. He had mapped the timeline. He had even allowed himself — in the quiet stretch between 4 AM and dawn, when the house was so still you could hear the refrigerator humming two rooms away — to feel something dangerously close to relief.

The problem with plans, as any toy who'd survived three owners and a garbage incinerator could tell you, was that children didn't read them.

The morning had started normally enough. Bonnie and Dennis had woken up, eaten breakfast (pancakes, based on the maple syrup smell drifting up the stairs), and returned to the bedroom for what Bonnie's mom had called "one more hour of play before Dennis's mom gets here." The crossover playdate resumed — calmer this time, softer, the kind of playing that happens on the second day when the novelty has faded and the friendship has deepened. Bonnie made Tanjiro and Jessie have a lasso competition. Dennis staged a philosophical debate between Goku and Rex about whether dinosaurs could learn martial arts. It was gentle. It was nice.

And then Bonnie's mom called from downstairs:

Bonnie's Mom: "Kids! Dennis's mom is here early! She's got a dentist appointment at eleven, so we need to wrap up in ten minutes!"

Ten minutes.

The children scrambled. Bonnie started collecting toys from the elaborate battlefield they'd created — a sprawling diorama that had migrated from the bedroom to the hallway to the top of the stairs over the course of the morning. Dennis grabbed his backpack. And in the chaos of two seven-year-olds trying to clean up a two-day playdate in the time it takes to microwave popcorn, something happened that made Woody's painted heart stop.

They forgot.

Not all of them. Dennis grabbed Goku from the desk — muscle memory, probably, since Goku was his favorite — and shoved him into the backpack. He found Naruto on the bookshelf (still sitting among the ruins of the second pencil outpost, which had been rebuilt and re-destroyed during the morning's play session) and tossed him in too. But the others —

Luffy was on the kitchen counter. Dennis had brought him downstairs during breakfast to "guard the pancakes" and never brought him back up.

Ichigo was on the living room windowsill. He'd been placed there during a game where "the Soul Reaper guards the castle" — the castle being the TV remote — and had been left behind when the game migrated upstairs.

Deku was in the bathroom. Bonnie had decided he needed "a bath" during an elaborate spa scene, and he was currently sitting on the edge of the sink with a tiny washcloth draped over his shoulders like a toga.

And Tanjiro — Tanjiro was in the backyard. On the porch. In the planter box next to the geraniums. Dennis had staged a "Demon Slayer versus the Garden Monster" scene through the back door and had propped Tanjiro against a flowerpot and then gotten distracted by a butterfly and never gone back for him.

Four figures. Four locations. One house. And a mother's car idling in the driveway with a dentist appointment that would not wait.

Dennis: "Mom! MOM! I can't find Luffy! Or Ichigo! Or —"

Dennis's Mom: (from downstairs, keys jangling) "Honey, we're late. Bonnie's mom said she'll find them and we can pick them up next time. Let's GO."

Dennis: "But —"

Dennis's Mom: "Dennis. Car. Now."

The front door closed. The car pulled away. And in Bonnie's bedroom, Woody lay on the bed where Bonnie had tossed him during the cleanup, staring at the ceiling with the expression of a sheriff who had just watched four members of his temporary jurisdiction get scattered across enemy territory with no extraction plan.

The room was quiet for exactly three seconds.

Woody: "Emergency meeting. Now."

🚨 ⭐ 🚨

Bonnie's mom was downstairs cleaning up the kitchen. Bonnie had been deposited in front of the TV with a juice box and a nature documentary about penguins. That gave them — Woody estimated, based on thirty years of predicting adult behavior — somewhere between twenty and forty minutes before someone came upstairs.

It was enough. It had to be enough.

The toys assembled by the toy chest. Bonnie's crew knew the drill — Buzz to Woody's right, Jessie to his left, Slinky and Hamm flanking, Rex hovering nervously behind. Dolly, Trixie, Buttercup, and Pricklepants formed the second row. Forky stood slightly apart, as always, vibrating with the confused energy of someone who wasn't entirely sure why they existed but was committed to the experience.

Goku and Naruto — the only two anime figures Dennis had managed to grab — sat on the desk. They'd been placed in the backpack, then removed when Dennis's mom had said "just grab the backpack, we'll sort it at home" and Dennis had upended everything looking for his water bottle. They were technically still Dennis's toys, temporarily in Bonnie's room, and they both knew it.

Woody: "Here's the situation. Four of the anime figures are scattered across the house. Luffy's in the kitchen. Ichigo's in the living room. Deku's in the bathroom. And Tanjiro —" He paused, because the next part was the one that made his pull-string coil. "Tanjiro is outside. In the backyard. In a planter box."

Jessie: "OUTSIDE?!"

Woody: "Outside."

Hamm: "That's a code red. That's an actual code red. How long has he been out there?"

Woody: "Since yesterday afternoon. Dennis staged a garden battle through the back door and forgot to bring him in."

Rex: "Yesterday?! He's been outside all night?! In the WEATHER?! With ANIMALS?! With —" Rex's tiny arms flailed. "— with NATURE?!"

Buzz: "Rex. Focus." He turned to Woody, and Woody saw it — the shift. The click. The thing that happened behind Buzz's eyes when the situation required precision and he stopped being Bonnie's toy and became, for just a moment, the Space Ranger he'd once believed he was. "What's the plan, Sheriff?"

Woody had already worked it out. He'd been working it out since the car pulled away, running scenarios the way he always did — mapping routes, calculating risks, weighing the one variable that made every toy mission dangerous: the humans.

Woody: "We do this in pairs. Fast. Quiet. No heroics." He looked directly at Goku. "No. Heroics."

Goku: "Hey, I just sit really well now. That's my whole thing."

Woody: "Good. Keep it that way." He drew the routes in the air with his hand, muscle memory from a hundred toy rescues. "Team One: Buzz and Jessie — you've got Ichigo in the living room. The TV is on, which means Bonnie is in there. You'll need to extract him without being seen. Jessie, you know Bonnie's attention patterns. Buzz, you know the floor plan. In and out."

Jessie: "I can work with that. Bonnie always falls asleep during nature documentaries. If it's penguins, we've got maybe fifteen minutes once her eyes close."

Woody: "Team Two: Hamm and Slinky — Luffy, kitchen counter. Bonnie's mom is in there cleaning, so timing is everything. Wait for her to take out the trash — she always does, it's Sunday — and grab him."

Slinky: "Counter's high. We'll need a boost."

Hamm: "I've got approximately four hundred and thirty-seven pennies' worth of structural integrity. I can be a step stool."

Woody: "Team Three: Dolly and Trixie — Deku, bathroom. He's on the sink. The bathroom's upstairs, so the distance is short, but the floor is tile and you'll slide. Be careful."

Dolly: "Tile floors. My nemesis. I've lost more dignity on bathroom tile than I care to discuss."

Trixie: "I'll handle the traction. Plastic feet on tile — I was literally designed for smooth surfaces."

Woody: "And Team Four..." He paused. "Team Four is the problem."

Everyone knew why. Tanjiro was outside. Outside meant the back door. The back door meant crossing the kitchen (where Bonnie's mom was), going through the mudroom, opening the screen door, navigating the porch, and reaching a planter box that was approximately three feet off the ground and exposed to the full view of the kitchen window.

It was, by any measure, the hardest extraction of Woody's career. And he'd once rescued a toy from a moving airplane.

Woody: "Team Four is me." He looked at Goku and Naruto. "And them."

The room went silent.

Naruto: "...Us?"

Woody: "You're the only ones who know Tanjiro. Who he'd respond to. Who he'd trust. If I show up alone, he might freeze wrong or panic. If his people show up..." He met Goku's eyes. "He'll know what's happening. And he'll know what to do."

It was the first time Woody had called them "his people." He didn't realize it until after he'd said it. But it was true, and everyone in the room knew it.

Goku: "You want us to run a rescue mission. Without powers. Without abilities. Without anything except —"

Woody: "Except each other. Yeah." He adjusted his hat — the gesture he always made before impossible things. "Welcome to being a toy."

🗺️ ⭐ 🗺️

They moved at 10:47 AM.

Woody had learned, across decades of toy operations, that the key to moving through a human house wasn't speed — it was rhythm. You learned the rhythms. The creak of the third stair that meant someone was going up. The hum of the dishwasher that meant the kitchen was empty. The click-pause-click of Bonnie's mom's shoes on hardwood that told you exactly where she was and how fast she was moving.

Today, the rhythm was this: Bonnie on the couch, eyes heavy, penguin documentary murmuring. Bonnie's mom in the kitchen, running water, cupboards opening and closing. The back door — the one Woody needed — was through the kitchen, past the mudroom, three feet of exposure. Maybe four.

Teams One, Two, and Three deployed through the bedroom door in sequence. Buzz and Jessie went first — down the stairs, hugging the baseboard, silent as shadows. Hamm and Slinky followed thirty seconds later, Slinky's coils compressed tight to prevent jingling. Dolly and Trixie went last, slipping into the upstairs bathroom with the practiced ease of toys who'd been navigating tile floors since the Obama administration.

Woody watched them go. Then he turned to his team.

Goku and Naruto stood at the top of the stairs. They looked — and this was the part that caught Woody off guard — scared. Not the loud, dramatic fear of a monster-under-the-bed scenario. The quiet, real fear of someone who understood the stakes and didn't have the power to change them.

Naruto: (whispering, which for Naruto was approximately normal speaking volume) "I've never done this before. The stealth thing. I mean, I'm a ninja, but —"

Woody: "But you've never been this small and this fragile and this visible. I know." He put a hand on Naruto's shoulder — a gesture he'd learned from Buzz, who'd learned it from watching Andy's dad on old home videos. "First rule: stay low. Second rule: stay together. Third rule: if a human sees us, we drop. Instantly. No exceptions. I don't care if you're mid-step, mid-jump, mid-anything — you become plastic. Understood?"

Goku: "Understood."

Naruto: "...Understood."

Woody: "Then let's go get your friend."

They descended the stairs in the gap between the third and fourth stair creaks — the dead zone where the wood was solid and the carpet was thickest. Woody led, because Woody always led, and because leading was the only thing that kept the fear from becoming paralysis. Goku moved behind him with a surprising lightness — his body was compact, well-balanced, and his factory-molded feet gripped the carpet better than Woody's flat boots. Naruto brought up the rear, and while his stealth left something to be desired (he bumped the banister twice), his determination was absolute.

At the bottom of the stairs, Woody held up a hand. Stop. Listen.

From the living room: the narrator's voice on the penguin documentary, explaining something about Antarctic feeding patterns. From the couch: silence. Bonnie was asleep. Good. Jessie had called it.

From the kitchen: running water. Bonnie's mom was at the sink, her back to the hallway. The path to the mudroom ran along the kitchen's left wall — a strip of floor between the counter and the doorframe, maybe eighteen inches wide, fully exposed for about four seconds at a dead sprint.

Four seconds. Three toys. One chance.

Woody: (barely audible) "When she turns on the disposal — the garbage disposal is loud, it'll cover any sound — we go. Single file. Don't stop."

They waited. Woody counted Bonnie's mom's movements by sound: plate into dishwasher (clink), another plate (clink), glass (higher clink), scraping food scraps into the disposal —

The disposal roared to life. The grinding, metallic drone filled the kitchen like a small earthquake.

Woody: "GO."

Three toys sprinted across four feet of exposed kitchen floor. Woody's boots hit the linoleum with tiny, rapid taps. Goku moved like — well, like a fighter, even without powers, his center of gravity low, his steps precise. Naruto ran the way Naruto did everything: with maximum effort and minimum subtlety, his arms pumping in the distinctive ninja-run posture that, at six inches tall, was more endearing than aerodynamic.

They made the mudroom. The disposal cut off. Bonnie's mom didn't turn around.

Woody's heart — or the mechanism inside him that served the same purpose — was hammering. He looked at Goku and Naruto, crouched behind a pair of rain boots, and saw in their painted eyes something he recognized: the exhilaration. The terrifying, electric, addictive thrill of a mission in progress. They'd felt it. For the first time since losing their powers, they'd felt something that mattered.

Goku: (grinning — the real grin, the battle grin, the one that had nothing to do with comfort and everything to do with being alive) "That was fun."

Woody: "That was the easy part."

🏃 ⭐ 🏃

The back door was a screen door with a latch that sat three feet off the ground.

For a human, it was nothing. For three six-inch toys, it was Everest with a handle. But Woody had opened doors before — he'd learned the technique from Slinky years ago, using body weight and leverage to work latches that were designed for hands twenty times his size.

Goku braced himself against the doorframe. Naruto climbed onto his shoulders (an act that required more trust than either of them would have been capable of twenty-four hours ago). And Woody climbed both of them, reaching the latch with his pull-string ring hooked around the handle. One pull. Two. The latch clicked. The screen door swung open an inch — enough.

The morning hit them like a wall.

For toys who'd spent their entire existence indoors, the outside world was overwhelming. The sky was vast and close. The sounds — birds, wind, distant traffic, a lawnmower three houses over — were raw and unfiltered, not muffled by walls and windows. The sun was direct, warm, almost aggressive in its brightness. And the smells — grass and dirt and geraniums and something sweet and green that didn't have a name but was undeniably, unmistakably alive — filled the air with a richness that no room, no matter how full of toys, could replicate.

Naruto inhaled. Or tried to. His plastic body didn't have lungs, but the gesture was instinctive and deeply human.

Naruto: "It smells like... morning. Like actual morning. Not room-morning. Real morning."

Woody: "Focus. We've got maybe five minutes before she finishes the dishes and comes to check the back door."

They crossed the porch. It was made of painted wood — white, peeling slightly at the edges, warm from the sun. The planter box sat at the far end, a rectangle of dark soil and bright geraniums, and there — leaning against a terracotta pot, half hidden by leaves, his checkered haori dusted with a fine layer of pollen —

Tanjiro.

He was still. Perfectly, completely still. The morning light caught his painted scar, his dark red hair, the tiny sword strapped to his back. He looked like exactly what he was: a beautifully crafted action figure left in a garden overnight, touched by weather and time and the gentle indifference of nature.

And then his eyes moved. Just barely. Just enough.

Tanjiro: (softly, without moving his lips more than a millimeter) "...I knew you'd come."

Goku: "Of course we came. What did you think we were gonna do, leave you with the flowers?"

Tanjiro: "I thought about it. Staying. The geraniums are very peaceful. And last night..." He tilted his head slightly toward the sky. "I saw the stars. Real ones. Not stickers. Not glow-in-the-dark plastic. Real stars, Goku. Thousands of them. More than I've ever seen, even in my memories of the mountain. I just... sat here, and I watched them, and I thought: this is what it feels like to be small in a world that's very, very large. And it was... beautiful."

Woody watched this exchange and felt something shift inside him — the same shift he'd felt when Buzz had first understood what it meant to be Andy's toy. Tanjiro wasn't damaged. He wasn't traumatized by a night alone in the elements. He had done what Tanjiro always did: he had found the beauty in the difficulty. He had sat in a planter box and watched the stars and come out the other side with something that looked like peace.

Woody: "That's... real pretty, Tanjiro. And I mean that. But we need to get you inside before Bonnie's mom sees a toy in her geraniums and starts asking questions I can't answer."

Tanjiro: "Of course." He stood, brushed the pollen from his haori with small, careful movements, and bowed to the geraniums. "Thank you for the company."

Naruto: "Did he just bow to flowers?"

Goku: "Yep."

Naruto: "...Respect."

🌿 ⭐ 🌿

The return trip was harder.

Not because the route had changed — it was the same mudroom, same kitchen, same exposed strip of linoleum — but because now they were four instead of three, and Bonnie's mom had finished the dishes and was wiping counters, which meant she was mobile, unpredictable, and facing in their direction every eight to twelve seconds as she moved in her cleaning pattern.

Woody calculated. Timed her movements. Watched the rhythm.

Counter. Turn. Sink. Turn. Counter. Turn to fridge. Back to counter. There — the gap when she opened the fridge door. The door blocked her sightline to the mudroom entrance for approximately three seconds.

Three seconds. Four toys.

Woody: (mouthing) "Fridge. Door. Go."

The fridge door opened. Bonnie's mom leaned in, scanning shelves, murmuring something about leftover pasta.

Four toys bolted.

Woody first. Goku second, carrying Tanjiro piggyback because Tanjiro's pollen-dusted feet had no grip on linoleum. Naruto last, running in that ridiculous arms-back sprint, his headband catching the light from the kitchen window for one heart-stopping millisecond —

The fridge door closed.

They were in the hallway. All four. Bonnie's mom hadn't seen a thing.

At the base of the stairs, they found the other teams already assembled. Buzz and Jessie had Ichigo — extracted from the living room windowsill during a particularly dramatic scene in the penguin documentary where Bonnie had been so absorbed she didn't notice two toys army-crawling across the carpet behind the couch. Hamm and Slinky had Luffy — pulled from the kitchen counter using a technique that involved Slinky stretching to his maximum extension while Hamm served as a counterweight, a maneuver they'd perfected during the Great Pantry Incident of 2019.

And Dolly and Trixie had Deku. He still had the tiny washcloth on his shoulders. He looked like a miniature Roman senator.

Deku: (looking at the washcloth) "I'd like to keep this, actually. It's very soft."

Dolly: "It's a face cloth from the guest bathroom."

Deku: "It's a cape."

All eighteen toys. Twelve veterans and six newcomers. Assembled at the base of the stairs, looking up at the fourteen-step climb back to Bonnie's room like it was the final ascent of a mountain they'd been climbing all weekend.

Buzz: "All teams reporting. All targets recovered. Zero human detections. Mission status: success."

Woody: "Not yet. We're still exposed. Everyone upstairs. Now. Fast and quiet."

They climbed. Eighteen toys ascending a staircase in near-perfect silence — a feat of coordination that would have been impossible forty-eight hours ago when half of them couldn't agree on whose side of a blue crayon line the carpet belonged to.

Luffy helped Rex up the last step. Ichigo held the bedroom door for Dolly. Naruto spotted Forky when he almost toppled backwards off the seventh stair. Tanjiro — still dusted with pollen, still carrying the scent of geraniums — walked beside Woody the entire way, matching his pace, covering his blind side, the way a partner would.

They made it. All of them. The bedroom door clicked shut and Woody leaned against it with his hat over his face and his pull-string dangling and the kind of exhaustion that only came from caring too much about too many people in too dangerous a situation.

Jessie: "Woody? You okay?"

Woody: "I'm fine. I just... need a second."

He took the second. Then he took his hat off and looked at the room — the full room, all eighteen of them, arranged in a loose circle around the toy chest with the morning light streaming through the butterfly curtains and the glow-in-the-dark stars fading on the ceiling above.

The blue crayon line was still on the carpet. But nobody was standing on their side of it anymore. The anime figures were mixed in with Bonnie's toys like they'd always been there — Goku sitting next to Buzz, Naruto arguing with Hamm about something, Luffy leaning against Rex, Ichigo standing by the window but facing in for the first time, Deku showing Pricklepants his index card calculations, Tanjiro offering Forky a petal from the geraniums.

The line was there, but the border was gone.

🏠 ⭐ 🏠

It was Goku who said it first.

Goku: "That was the best fight I've had since I got here."

Ichigo: "It wasn't a fight. It was a rescue mission."

Goku: "Same energy. The heart-pounding, the timing, the teamwork, the moment when everything could go wrong but doesn't because the people beside you are exactly where they need to be." He cracked his knuckles — a gesture that produced no sound from plastic fingers but carried all the weight of habit. "I haven't felt that since the Tournament of Power. And I didn't need a single ki blast to feel it."

Naruto: "We ran a mission. An actual mission. Through enemy territory. With no jutsu. No shadow clones. Just —" He gestured broadly at the group. "— US. And we did it. WE DID IT. BELIEVE IT!"

Hamm: "He's back to yelling."

Naruto: "I will NEVER stop yelling! Yelling is my BIRTHRIGHT!"

Luffy: "I was on the kitchen counter all night and nobody thought to bring me a snack."

Jessie: "You were next to the fruit bowl."

Luffy: "Fruit isn't a snack. Fruit is what snacks eat."

The laughter was real. Not the cautious, testing laughter of strangers sharing space — the open, unguarded laughter of people who'd been through something together and come out the other side. It filled the room the way sunlight filled the room: completely, without asking permission, warming everything it touched.

Woody watched it. Leaned against the toy chest and watched the room he'd protected for years do the thing it had always done best — absorb someone new. Make space. Expand without breaking. The way it had absorbed Jessie, and Buzz, and Forky, and every terrified new toy who'd ever arrived in a child's room and wondered if there was room for one more.

There was always room for one more. That was the secret. That was the whole secret.

Buzz: (leaning close to Woody) "You did good, cowboy."

Woody: "We did good." He looked at Buzz — his partner, his friend, the space toy who'd crashed into his life and rearranged everything. "All of us."

Buzz: "Dennis's mom is coming back this afternoon to pick up the rest of his toys."

The words landed softly. But they landed.

Woody: "...I know."

Buzz: "They'll have to go back."

Woody: "I know that too."

They stood together in the morning light — a cowboy and a space ranger, watching six warriors who had become six friends, knowing that the goodbye was coming, knowing that the clock was running, knowing all the things that leaders always knew and carried and never said out loud because saying them would make them real and real was something you only faced when you absolutely had to.

But not yet. Right now, in this moment, the room was whole. The mission was complete. The border was gone. And somewhere between the panic and the planning and the three-second sprint across a kitchen floor, something had happened that Woody hadn't expected and couldn't quite name but recognized with the certainty of a toy who'd been loved by two children and survived every kind of loss:

They were a team now. All of them. Not two groups sharing space — one group, one crew, one ridiculous, impossible, beautiful collection of plastic and fabric and paint and feeling that had no business working together and did anyway.

I've led a lot of rescues. I've crossed yards and highways and conveyor belts and the back seat of a moving car. I've been stitched back together and pulled apart and dropped from heights that would make a human dizzy. And I've learned exactly one thing from all of it:

The mission is never about the distance. It's about who you're willing to cross it for.

Today, I crossed a kitchen floor with a Saiyan and a ninja to rescue a Demon Slayer from a flower pot. And it was — hand to pull-string, honest to goodness — one of the finest moments of my life. Not because it was dramatic. Not because it was dangerous. Because of the look on Tanjiro's face when he saw us coming. The way he said, "I knew you'd come." Not hoped. Not wished. Knew.

That's what it means to be part of something. That's what it means to be a team. When someone is out there in a planter box in the dark, watching stars they were never supposed to see — and they know you're coming for them.

Dennis's mom is coming back at three. The anime guys will go home. Back to Dennis's room. Back to a world where we're just toys on different shelves. But I don't think "just" is the right word anymore. I don't think it ever was. — Woody
