Act I — The Arrival
Chapter Two

Six Warriors Walk Into a Bedroom

Point of View: Alternating

The orange box opened first.

Not gently. Not the way a lid lifts when gravity and a loose flap conspire to create something that resembles a natural accident. No — this lid was punched off. It flew upward, spinning like a tiny cardboard Frisbee, and landed three inches away on the carpet with a soft, decisive pat.

For one full second, nothing happened.

Then a hand — small, plastic, painted the color of sun-warmed skin — reached out from inside the box and gripped the edge. The hand tensed. The cardboard creaked. And from the packaging foam and twist-ties and cellophane, a figure rose.

He was six inches tall. His hair — wild, black, sculpted into points that defied both gravity and good sense — stood straight up like a frozen explosion. He wore an orange martial arts uniform, the gi faithfully reproduced in miniature plastic, with a blue undershirt and a dark belt cinched tight around his waist. His boots were planted wide. His painted eyes were large and dark and impossibly alive, and they scanned the bedroom with the focused intensity of someone who was used to scanning battlefields.

Goku — Son Goku, Earth's greatest defender, the Saiyan who had punched gods and outrun light and died more times than most people changed socks — stood on the carpet of a seven-year-old girl's bedroom and looked around with an expression of genuine, cheerful confusion.

Goku: "Huh."

He stretched his arms overhead, rolled his neck, and bounced on his heels like a fighter warming up before a match. Then he looked down at himself — at the plastic joints, the painted seams, the little copyright stamp on his left boot — and his brow furrowed.

Goku: "Okay, this is new. Definitely new. I don't remember being... this small. Or this shiny." He knocked on his own chest. "Am I made of... plastic? That's... huh. That's weird."

Across the room, every single one of Bonnie's toys stared at him with the kind of frozen horror that came from watching a new arrival not only move on his own, but talk to himself about it out loud, like it was interesting instead of terrifying.

Rex: (whispering, barely audible) "He's... he's talking. The new toy is talking. He just got out of the box and he's already talking. Woody, is that normal? That's not normal. That is NOT normal."

Woody: (also whispering) "Rex. Breathe."

Rex: "I don't have lungs!"

Woody: "Then breathe symbolically."

📦 ⭐ 📦

The second box opened before anyone could process the first.

This one didn't punch its lid off — it exploded. Cellophane burst outward like confetti. Twist-ties snapped. The box itself fell apart along its fold lines as though it had been hit by a very small, very concentrated hurricane, and from the wreckage leaped a figure in an orange-and-black jumpsuit with a headband tied across his forehead — a metal plate engraved with a leaf symbol glinting under the bedroom light.

Naruto Uzumaki landed in a crouch, one hand on the carpet, the other extended behind him in a pose that was half ninja, half action movie poster. His blonde plastic hair spiked outward. His painted whisker marks — three thin lines on each cheek — caught the light. And his eyes — bright, blue, fierce — locked onto the room with the evaluating stare of someone trained since childhood to assess any new environment for threats, exits, and ramen.

Naruto: "Where is this?! What happened?! I was just — I was in the middle of — who are —" He spotted Goku. "Wait. Who are YOU? Are you an enemy?! You look like you could be an enemy! You've got that 'enemy' vibe!"

Goku: "Me? Nah, I just got here too. I'm Goku. I think we're in a... room? Like, a kid's room?"

Naruto: "A KID'S room?! What kind of jutsu is this?! This is definitely a genjutsu. Someone put us in a genjutsu. RELEASE!" He clasped his hands together and shut his eyes hard. "...RELEASE!"

Nothing happened.

Naruto: "...Okay, so it's a really good genjutsu."

The third box opened sideways. The lid simply fell off — dropped flat like a drawbridge lowering — and from within, a figure stepped out with the unhurried confidence of someone entering his own living room. He wore a red vest, blue shorts, and sandals. A straw hat hung from a string around his neck. And his grin — wide, fearless, sculpted into the plastic with the unmistakable energy of a boy who had sailed every sea and laughed at every storm — lit up the room like a small sun.

Luffy: "Whoa. This place is HUGE." He looked up at the bed towering above him. "IS THAT A MOUNTAIN?! No, wait — it's a bed. That's a REALLY BIG bed. I bet you could fit forty people on that thing. Or at least one REALLY big dinner."

Goku: "You're thinking about food too? Man, I like you already."

Luffy: "I'm ALWAYS thinking about food! I'm Luffy! Monkey D. Luffy! And I'm gonna be King of the Pirates!"

Naruto: "Pirates?! Why would you want to be a pirate?! I'm gonna be HOKAGE — leader of the entire Hidden Leaf Village! That's way better than a pirate!"

Luffy: "Hoka-what? That sounds made up."

Naruto: "IT IS NOT MADE UP!"

Meanwhile, Bonnie's toys had not moved. They stood — or sat, or lay, depending on their position — in a state of collective paralysis that had gone from "frozen in shock" to "frozen in fascinated disbelief" and was now rapidly approaching "frozen because we genuinely have no idea what to do."

Hamm: (to Woody, through the side of his mouth) "Sheriff. I don't want to alarm you. But there are three action figures standing in the middle of the room arguing about career goals, and we haven't even gotten to boxes four, five, and six."

⚔️ ⭐ ⚔️

Box four opened like a coffin.

That was the only way Woody could describe it. The lid rose slowly — vertically, defying the physics of cardboard — and from the dark interior, a figure emerged standing upright. He was taller than the others by a fraction. He wore black robes — a shihakusho, though nobody in the room knew that word — with a white sash at his waist. His hair was bright orange, shocking against the black fabric, and his expression was the opposite of everything that had come before: no confusion, no excitement, no grinning. Just a sharp, evaluating calm — the face of someone who had fought monsters in the dark and had stopped being surprised by anything a long time ago.

Ichigo Kurosaki looked at the bedroom. Looked at the three figures already standing on the carpet. Looked at the ring of frozen toys watching from the walls and shelves. Then he looked down at his own six-inch plastic body and exhaled through his nose.

Ichigo: "...Great. This is just great."

Goku: "Hey! Welcome! Do you know where we are?"

Ichigo: "A child's bedroom. Probably on the mortal plane. Possibly a pocket dimension. Either way —" He flexed his plastic fingers. "— my zanpakutō is gone, my reiatsu is zero, and I appear to be made of injection-molded thermoplastic. So I'd say the situation is... less than ideal."

Luffy: "You're kinda gloomy, huh?"

Ichigo: "I'm not gloomy. I'm realistic."

Luffy: "That's what gloomy people always say."

The fifth box didn't open. It trembled. For a long, agonizing moment, the lid twitched and shook and bulged — and then, with a soft, apologetic pop, it tilted open just enough for a pair of wide green eyes to peer out.

The eyes blinked. They looked left. They looked right. They looked at the four figures already standing in formation, and then they looked at the crowd of unfamiliar toys watching from every surface, and they widened with the unmistakable expression of someone who was very smart, very nervous, and trying very hard to figure out what was happening before committing to any course of action.

Izuku Midoriya — Deku to everyone who knew him, to everyone he'd ever saved, to the voice in his head that still sounded like All Might on his best day — pushed the lid open the rest of the way and climbed out of the box with the careful, deliberate movements of someone who was used to being underestimated and had learned to make every step count.

He wore his green hero suit — the full-body costume with the hood pulled back, the metal mouth guard hanging loose around his neck. His hair was a wild green tangle. His freckled face was earnest and alert. And in his painted eyes, behind the anxiety, there was something that Woody recognized instantly — because he'd seen it in a space ranger's eyes once, a long time ago, in a boy's bedroom not so different from this one.

He believes.

Deku: "E-excuse me. I don't mean to interrupt, but... does anyone know what's going on? Because I've been running calculations since I woke up and none of the variables make sense. My Quirk isn't responding, my limbs feel... rigid, and the proportions of this room suggest we're approximately one-twelfth scale, which would make us —"

Naruto: "Tiny. We're tiny, kid. Welcome to the weirdness."

Deku: "Right. Tiny. Okay." He took a breath — or the plastic equivalent of one. "I can work with tiny. Tiny is just... a constraint. Constraints are just problems with boundaries. And problems with boundaries are —"

Ichigo: "Still problems?"

Deku: "— solvable. I was going to say solvable."

The sixth and final box opened in silence.

No punching, no explosion, no trembling. The lid simply lifted — gently, respectfully, the way you might open a door into someone else's home — and a figure stepped out with a quiet grace that made every other entrance look like a bar fight.

He was wearing a green-and-black checkered haori over a dark uniform. Hanafuda earrings — tiny, painted with meticulous detail — hung from his ears. His burgundy hair was pulled back, and a scar on his forehead caught the morning light. But it was his eyes that held the room — calm, kind, the color of dark water, carrying the steady warmth of someone who had walked through fire and come out the other side not harder, but gentler.

Tanjiro Kamado looked around the bedroom the way a visitor looks at a temple — with attention, with care, with the instinct to remove his shoes even though he was only six inches tall and his shoes were molded onto his feet.

Tanjiro: "This place... it smells like sunlight and crayons. And something sweet — like the inside of a child's laugh."

Ichigo: "You can smell a laugh?"

Tanjiro: "Of course. Can't you?"

Ichigo: "No. Nobody can. That's not a thing."

Tanjiro: "Hmm. That's a shame. You're missing quite a lot."

He turned his gaze to the Bonnie's toys — to the semicircle of frozen, wide-eyed figures who had been watching this parade of impossibilities unfold — and he did something that none of the other newcomers had done.

He bowed.

Tanjiro: "My name is Tanjiro Kamado. I apologize for the intrusion. We seem to have arrived without invitation, and I can see that this is your home. Please forgive us."

The room held its breath. Every toy on every shelf stared at the bowing figure in the checkered coat. Behind Tanjiro, the other five anime figures stood in a loose cluster — Goku with his arms crossed and a friendly grin, Naruto still scanning the room like a caged fox, Luffy reaching up toward a shelf he couldn't reach (likely spotted a button he thought was candy), Ichigo standing apart with his arms folded, and Deku quietly muttering calculations under his breath.

And in front of them all, between the two groups, Woody stepped forward.

🤠 ⭐ 🤠

Sheriff Woody Pride had met a lot of new toys in his life. He had watched them arrive in birthday wrapping and Christmas stockings and Amazon boxes and, once, memorably, in a claw machine at Pizza Planet. He had welcomed some with open arms and greeted others with suspicion. He had been jealous of them and protective of them and occasionally terrified of them. He had a speech for this — a welcome speech, refined over years, full of reassurance and room assignments and the gentle explanation that yes, you are a toy, and no, that's not a bad thing, and yes, the kid loves you, and no, the dinosaur won't stop screaming, that's just Rex.

But none of those speeches had been written for this.

Six figures from a world he'd never heard of, speaking languages of combat and destiny and dreams so large they made "to infinity and beyond" sound like a modest weekend plan. Six figures who had punched their own boxes open, who were arguing about being kings and leaders before they'd even looked around properly, who smelled laughs and ran calculations and treated the entire situation with a casual intensity that suggested being teleported into a child's bedroom was, for them, maybe a four out of ten on the weirdness scale.

Woody adjusted his hat. Took a breath. And did what a sheriff does.

Woody: "Alright. Howdy."

Six pairs of painted eyes turned to look at him.

Woody: "My name is Woody. Sheriff Woody Pride. I'm the — well, I'm sort of the... person in charge around here. This is Bonnie's room. She's seven. She's great. She's currently outside drinking lemonade, so we've got a little time." He paused. "Now. I'm going to be straight with you, because that's how I do things. I don't know who you are, I don't know where you came from, and I definitely don't know how you just opened your own boxes from the inside, because in thirty-plus years of being a toy, I have never seen that happen."

Goku: "Wait — did you say toy?"

The word landed like a stone in a still pond.

Goku: "What do you mean, toy? I'm not a toy. I'm a Saiyan. I'm a martial artist. I've fought Frieza. I've fought Buu. I've fought Beerus. I don't —" He looked at his plastic hands again, turning them slowly in the light. "...I'm not a toy."

Naruto: "Yeah! I'm a ninja! A SHINOBI! I've got the Nine-Tails inside me — well, I had the Nine-Tails inside me — well, it's complicated, but the POINT is, I'm NOT a toy! Believe it!"

Luffy: "I'm rubber! I ate the Gum-Gum Fruit! Watch —" He grabbed his own arm and pulled. "...Why isn't my arm stretching? My arm ALWAYS stretches. What happened to my arm?!"

Ichigo: "Your arm didn't stretch because you're made of ABS plastic and articulated at the shoulder, elbow, and wrist joints. Whatever we were before, right now... we're action figures."

A silence fell. The kind of silence that happens when someone says the thing that everyone was thinking but nobody wanted to be the first to admit.

Deku: (quietly) "He's right. I've been trying to activate One For All since I woke up. Nothing. No power. No Quirk. Not even the tingles. It's like... it was never there."

Tanjiro: "My breathing techniques... I can feel the memory of them. Like an echo. But when I try to perform Water Breathing, nothing happens. My body knows the forms, but the energy isn't there."

Goku sat down on the carpet. Not dramatically — not in defeat — but in the way a fighter sits between rounds, conserving energy, processing the situation. The other five figures looked at him, and for the first time, the boisterous energy that had filled the room dimmed to something quieter. Something human.

Something fragile.

Goku: "So... no Ki. No transformations. No Kamehameha." He was quiet for a moment. "That's... okay. I've been powerless before. It's just been a while."

Something shifted in the room right then. Woody felt it — a frequency change, like a radio finding a new station. These weren't just noisy new arrivals. These were warriors who had just lost everything they knew about themselves — their power, their size, their world — and were sitting on a stranger's carpet trying to figure out who they were without the one thing that had always defined them. — Narrative
🌟 ⭐ 🌟

Buzz Lightyear had been watching from the shelf.

He had not spoken during the unboxing. He had not gasped or whispered or nudged anyone. He had simply watched — with the careful, methodical attention of someone who understood, better than perhaps anyone else in this room, exactly what the six newcomers were going through.

Because Buzz remembered.

He remembered the day he had landed in Andy's room, convinced he was a real space ranger. He remembered flying — or rather, falling with style — off the bed and hitting the floor and looking at the sticker on his wrist and the seam on his arm and the MADE IN TAIWAN stamp on the inside of his thigh. He remembered the exact second when "Buzz Lightyear of Star Command" became "Buzz Lightyear, toy." He remembered the fall. Not the physical one. The other one — the one inside, where the floor drops out and you realize that everything you believed about yourself was a story someone else wrote on the side of your box.

That fall had nearly broken him.

And now he was watching six strangers take that same fall, all at the same time, in a room that wasn't theirs, surrounded by faces they didn't know.

Buzz climbed down from the shelf. His boots clicked softly on the hardwood. Every eye in the room — toy and action figure alike — turned to follow him as he walked past Woody, past Jessie, past the semicircle of Bonnie's toys, and stopped directly in front of Goku, who was still sitting on the carpet.

Buzz extended his hand.

Buzz: "Buzz Lightyear. Space Ranger. Or... I was. That's what it says on my box, anyway." He paused. "I know what you're feeling right now. All of you. I know the exact shape of it — the confusion, the disbelief, the part of your brain that keeps insisting this can't be real because you remember being more than this. I've been there. I've stood exactly where you're standing."

Goku looked at the extended hand. Looked at Buzz — at the purple-and-white armor, the translucent green visor folded back, the small, serious face with its painted jaw and steady eyes. And something passed between them — a recognition. Not of names or worlds or powers, but of something deeper.

The recognition of someone who had once lost everything they thought they were, and had found something worth being on the other side.

Goku grinned. It wasn't the wide, reckless grin from before — it was smaller, quieter, realer. The grin of a man who had been knocked down more times than he could count and who always, always got back up.

Goku: "Space Ranger, huh? That sounds pretty cool."

He took Buzz's hand and shook it.

Goku: "I'm Goku. And I don't really know what I am right now. But... it's nice to meet someone who gets it."

Behind Buzz, Woody watched the handshake — and felt something he hadn't felt in a long time. Not worry. Not jealousy. Not the old, territorial prickle that used to rise in his chest whenever a new toy showed up and threatened to change the order of things.

What he felt was pride. Pure, uncomplicated pride — in his partner, in his friend, in the space ranger who had once been as lost as these six strangers and had somehow turned that loss into the foundation of something extraordinary.

That's my guy, Woody thought. That's my co-pilot. Go get 'em, Buzz.

🚀 ⭐ 🚀

What followed was the most chaotic round of introductions in the history of Bonnie's bedroom.

Woody tried to maintain order. He really did. He started with the standard approach — name, role, brief description of how things worked around here — but the anime figures had a fundamentally different relationship with concepts like "order" and "waiting your turn."

Woody: "So, this is Jessie — she's a cowgirl, part of the Roundup gang, and she's —"

Luffy: "A cowgirl! Like a cowboy but a girl! That's SO COOL! Do you ride cows?!"

Jessie: (blinking) "Horses, actually. I ride —"

Luffy: "CAN I RIDE A HORSE?! Where are the horses?! Are they in that box over there?!"

Jessie: "That's... a Kleenex box."

Luffy: "Is there a horse inside it?"

Jessie: "...No."

Luffy: "Are you SURE?"

Naruto, meanwhile, had found Rex and was circling him with the intense focus of a hunter evaluating prey — or possibly a very excited child encountering his first dinosaur.

Naruto: "WHOA! Is this a SUMMONING ANIMAL?! He's HUGE! Is he yours?! Can he fight?! What jutsu does he know?!"

Rex: "I — I'm Rex. I'm a tyrannosaurus. I don't know any joot-soo. I don't even know what joot-soo IS. I know how to play Buzz Lightyear: Attack on Zurg on the Super Nintendo, and I'm pretty good at it, but that's — that's really more of a hobby than a —"

Naruto: "CAN YOU BREATHE FIRE?!"

Rex: "NO! I can barely breathe at ALL when people shout at me!"

On the dresser, Hamm was having a very different kind of conversation with Ichigo.

Hamm: "So let me get this straight. In your world, you fight ghosts."

Ichigo: "Hollows. They're called Hollows."

Hamm: "Ghosts with masks."

Ichigo: "...Technically accurate."

Hamm: "With a sword."

Ichigo: "A zanpakutō. It's a spiritual weapon that —"

Hamm: "A sword."

Ichigo: (sighing) "...A sword."

Hamm: "And you're a teenager."

Ichigo: "Fifteen. Or I was."

Hamm: "A fifteen-year-old ghost fighter with a sword. And I thought the space ranger was complicated."

Across the room, Deku had gravitated — inevitably, magnetically — toward Mr. Pricklepants, and the two of them were already deep in a conversation that nobody else could follow.

Deku: "— and so if the fundamental rules of physics are different here, then the question becomes: is our consciousness native to these bodies, or is it a transfer? Because if it's a transfer, there might be a return mechanism, but if we're native, then our memories of being human-sized are actually just... firmware? Pre-loaded backstory?"

Mr. Pricklepants: "What you're describing, dear boy, is essentially Pirandello's dilemma — are we the characters, or are we the actors who have forgotten we're performing? Six Characters in Search of an Author, 1921. Profoundly relevant."

Deku: "I haven't read that, but it sounds exactly right. Can I borrow it?"

Mr. Pricklepants: "You may borrow my soul if it means someone in this room finally appreciates dramatic theory."

And in the quietest corner of the room, beside the window where the morning light fell warm and golden, Tanjiro was kneeling next to Forky.

He had noticed the spork — noticed him in the way that Tanjiro noticed everyone, with the deep, instinctive attentiveness of someone whose greatest gift was not his sword or his breathing technique but his capacity to see people who were trying very hard not to be seen.

Tanjiro: "Hello. What's your name?"

Forky: "I'm Forky! I'm trash!"

Tanjiro: "I don't think you're trash."

Forky: "No, I really am! I used to be a spork! Bonnie made me in art class. I had a popsicle stick and some googly eyes and everything! I'm LITERALLY trash that became a toy!"

Tanjiro was quiet for a moment. He studied Forky — the mismatched googly eyes, the pipe-cleaner arms, the popsicle-stick feet, the entire cobbled-together existence of a creature who should not be alive and somehow, beautifully, impossibly, was.

Tanjiro: "Where I come from, Forky, there is a word — kintsugi. It means repairing broken things with gold. The idea is that when something breaks, you don't hide the cracks. You fill them with something precious, and the broken thing becomes more beautiful than it was before." He smiled gently. "You were a spork. Someone found you and made you into something more. That doesn't make you trash. That makes you kintsugi."

Forky: "...Kin-tsoo-gee?"

Tanjiro: "Kintsugi. It means you're not broken. You're golden."

Forky's googly eyes went very, very wide. His pipe-cleaner arms trembled. And for the first time in his short, bewildering, wonderful life, the spork-turned-toy looked at someone and didn't say "trash."

Forky: "...Golden?"

Tanjiro: "Golden."

Woody watched the exchange from across the room. He'd been preparing a speech — his standard "welcome to the room, here are the rules" speech, updated with emergency clauses for anime warriors — but he let it go. Some moments didn't need a sheriff. Some moments just needed witnesses.

I've been around long enough to know what real kindness looks like. It's not the big gestures — it's not the rescue missions or the dramatic speeches. It's kneeling down next to a spork in a quiet corner and telling him he's golden. I don't know where this Tanjiro kid came from. But I know a good soul when I see one. Even if that soul is made of injection-molded thermoplastic. — Woody, internal monologue
🌸 ⭐ 🌸

The peace lasted about twelve minutes.

It ended — as most things ended in rooms full of strong personalities and limited floor space — with a territorial dispute.

Luffy had climbed the bedpost. Not walked to the base and looked up at it longingly the way a toy might — climbed it, hand over plastic hand, with the scrambling enthusiasm of a boy who had scaled masts and sea kings and the Red Line itself. He reached the top, planted his feet on the headboard, and spread his arms wide.

Luffy: "I CLAIM THIS BED IN THE NAME OF THE STRAW HAT PIRATES! THIS IS NOW OUR SHIP! THE — THE —" He looked around for a name. "THE THOUSAND SUNNY... JUNIOR! Yeah! Thousand Sunny Junior! I'm the captain! Who wants to be my crew?!"

Dolly: "That's... that's Bonnie's bed."

Luffy: "It's a SHIP now!"

Dolly: "It is absolutely not a ship. It is a twin-size mattress with a fitted sheet and a comforter that has butterflies on it. It is where a seven-year-old sleeps."

Luffy: "A seven-year-old who sleeps ON A SHIP!"

Naruto, not to be outdone, had scaled the bookshelf and was arranging a row of erasers into what he loudly declared was "Mount Hokage" — a monument to the leaders of his village, represented by five colored erasers of slightly different sizes.

Naruto: "This eraser is the First Hokage! This one's the Second! This is the Third — Gramps, rest in peace — this is the Fourth — that's my DAD, by the way —"

Trixie: "Those are Bonnie's math erasers."

Naruto: "They're HOKAGE MONUMENTS now, believe it!"

Woody felt his left eye twitch. It was a subtle twitch — barely visible, just a faint pulse in the painted muscle above his cheekbone — but it was there. He recognized it. It was the same twitch he'd gotten the day Buzz had first arrived and started touching things. The same twitch from the Sunnyside Daycare incident. The same twitch that appeared, without fail, whenever someone threatened the careful, hard-won equilibrium of his world.

Woody: "Okay. Alright. Listen up — all of you. ALL of you."

The room quieted. Woody stepped to the center — the geographic and emotional center of Bonnie's bedroom — and when he spoke, it was in the voice that every toy in this room knew. Not the friendly voice. Not the welcoming voice. The sheriff's voice.

Woody: "I get it. You're confused. You're scared. You woke up in a place you don't know, in bodies you don't recognize, without the powers you're used to. That's a rough morning. Trust me, I've seen rough mornings. But here's the thing — this room has rules. And those rules exist for one reason: to keep Bonnie safe. To keep ALL of us safe. So before anybody claims any more beds or builds any more monuments, let me make one thing very clear:"

He paused. Made eye contact with each of the six newcomers, one by one.

Woody: "When the kids come back — and they will come back — nobody moves. Nobody talks. Nobody breathes. You are TOYS. And the first rule of being a toy is: humans can NEVER know you're alive. That's not a guideline. That's not a suggestion. That is the law. Break it, and you don't just endanger yourself — you endanger every toy in this room."

Silence.

Goku uncrossed his arms. Naruto stopped building. Luffy, still perched on the headboard, tilted his head like a curious bird. Ichigo's expression didn't change — but his posture shifted, just slightly, toward something that might have been respect. Deku was already nodding, clearly adding "don't let humans see you move" to his growing mental checklist. And Tanjiro — Tanjiro simply met Woody's eyes and gave a small, solemn nod.

Goku: "So... it's like being undercover?"

Woody: "It's exactly like being undercover. Except the mission never ends. And the person you're protecting can never know you're protecting them."

Goku considered this. Turned it over behind his eyes. And then he nodded — not the nod of a warrior accepting orders, but the nod of someone who understood the weight of a promise made to someone who would never know it was being kept.

Goku: "I get it. Protect the kid. Stay hidden. Don't blow the cover." He grinned — smaller this time, but warmer. "I've done harder. Probably."

Naruto: "Yeah... okay. I can do that. I was a ninja, after all. Stealth is kind of my thing." Beat. "Well. It's supposed to be my thing. I'm... working on it."

Jessie: (under her breath to Buzz) "The loud one says stealth is his thing."

Buzz: (under his breath back) "Give him time."

Luffy climbed slowly — reluctantly — down from the headboard. He looked at the bed one last time with the longing of a captain leaving his ship, and then turned to Woody.

Luffy: "Okay, Sheriff guy. I'll follow your rules. BUT —" He pointed at Woody with absolute conviction. "— that bed is STILL a ship. In my heart."

Woody: "...I can live with that."

And just like that — not perfectly, not completely, but enough — the twelve toys of Bonnie's room and the six warriors of another world reached something that resembled an understanding. Not a friendship. Not yet. Not even close. But an understanding — a thin, fragile line drawn on the carpet of a seven-year-old's bedroom that said: We are here. We are different. And for now, we are going to try.

Through the window, the sun climbed higher. Somewhere outside, Bonnie laughed. The backyard sprinkler ticked its steady rhythm. And in the room — in the beautiful, cluttered, complicated room — twelve old toys and six new figures stood together for the first time, on either side of a line that was already beginning to blur.

That's the thing about lines. You draw them to keep things separate, to keep things safe, to keep things in their place. But lines have a funny way of becoming bridges when you're not looking. And I've got a feeling — deep in my pull-string, all the way down to the knot — that the line we drew today isn't going to stay a line for long. — Woody
