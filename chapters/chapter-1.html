Act I — The Arrival
Chapter One

A Quiet Morning in Bonnie's Room

Point of View: Woody

The first light of Saturday morning came in slow, the way it always did — like honey sliding off a spoon. It crept across Bonnie Anderson's bedroom floor in a warm golden ribbon, climbing the edge of the toy chest, stretching lazily over the windowsill, and finally settling on the face of a cowboy doll propped against the far wall.

Woody didn't move. He never did — not when there was a chance, however small, that someone might be watching. That was Rule Number One. The first rule he'd ever learned, back when his world was a boy named Andy, a twin bed with rocket ship sheets, and a window that overlooked a quiet suburban street. Some things stayed with a toy forever. The shape of a child's hand. The warmth of being chosen. And the absolute, unshakeable discipline of going limp the instant the doorknob turned.

But nobody was watching now. The house was still. Bonnie was asleep down the hall, and the faint mechanical hum of the refrigerator downstairs was the only sound that drifted through the floorboards. Outside, a pair of blue jays argued in the old maple tree. A lawn sprinkler two houses over ticked like a metronome.

Saturday.

Woody let the stillness hold for another three seconds — that was his habit, always three extra seconds, just in case — and then, very gently, he turned his head.

Bonnie's room was its usual cheerful disaster. Crayon drawings were tacked to every available inch of wall. A half-finished watercolor of a castle sat drying on the desk next to a mason jar full of cloudy paint water. Three blankets of different patterns tangled together across the bed like a fabric coral reef. And everywhere — on shelves, on the floor, leaned against books, stuffed into a handmade cardboard "parking garage" — were toys.

His toys. Their toys. The family.

⭐ ⭐ ⭐

Dolly was already up. Of course she was. Dolly was always up first, moving with the quiet, purposeful energy of a dayshift manager at a very small, very disorganized hotel. She had Bonnie's collection of colored pencils sorted by shade and was now eyeing a stray sock on the floor with the kind of intensity usually reserved for defusing a bomb.

Dolly: "That sock has been there since Wednesday. Wednesday. I've counted four sunrises, two vacuumings that missed it by inches, and one near-catastrophe when Bonnie almost tripped on it. Somebody needs to nudge it under the bed tonight."

Woody: "It's a sock, Dolly."

Dolly: "It's a hazard, Woody. A cotton-polyester hazard sitting in the middle of the primary foot traffic lane."

Woody smiled. Dolly's neurotic tidiness had been grating at first — back when he was still adjusting to being a Bonnie's-room toy instead of an Andy's-room toy. But over time he'd come to appreciate it. She kept things running. She was the glue. Not the kind of glue that held plastic parts together, but the kind that held a community together, which was harder and more important.

Across the room, on the shelf next to a well-thumbed copy of The Velveteen Rabbit, Mr. Pricklepants was warming up. He stood on a stack of index cards that he had long ago designated as his "stage," and his voice carried the theatrical gravity of a hedgehog who believed — truly, deeply, in his stitched little heart — that he was Sir Laurence Olivier.

Mr. Pricklepants: "To be, or not to be — that is the question. Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune —"

Buttercup: "— or to shut up so the rest of us can enjoy a quiet morning? That's also a question."

Mr. Pricklepants: "You are a philistine, Buttercup."

Buttercup: "And you're a hedgehog doing Shakespeare at seven in the morning. We all have our crosses to bear."

Buttercup yawned — a long, performative yawn that showed every one of his plush unicorn teeth. He was positioned near the foot of the bed, in what he called his "observation post" and what everyone else called "the spot where he naps." To his credit, Buttercup's cynicism was never truly mean. It was the grumpy affection of a toy who had seen enough to know that the world was absurd, and who chose to find it funny rather than frightening.

Near the desk, Trixie sat propped against the keyboard of Bonnie's mom's old laptop, which Bonnie had inherited for "educational games" but which Trixie used, during the quiet hours, for things that were decidedly not educational. Her small plastic claws tapped noiselessly against the keys, and the screen's glow reflected off her blue plastic hide.

Trixie: "Update: VelociStar237 is not returning my messages. It has been eleven days. I'm starting to think the connection we had during the Jurassic Kart tournament finals was purely competitive."

Rex: "Maybe they're busy?"

Trixie: "Rex, nobody is 'busy' for eleven days. Even dinosaurs had social lives."

Rex: "Actually, most studies suggest dinosaurs had very limited social structures, except for some hadrosaurs and —"

Trixie: "Not helping, Rex."

Rex lowered his head apologetically. The large green tyrannosaurus was sitting in his usual spot near the toy box, his tiny arms folded in what he probably thought was a relaxed pose but what actually looked like a small dinosaur trying very hard not to fall over. Rex worried about everything — loud noises, small spaces, whether his roar was intimidating enough (it wasn't), and whether the newer toys secretly thought he was obsolete (they didn't). He was, in Woody's opinion, the most anxious dinosaur in any era, past or present.

Hamm sat on the dresser, watching a dust mote float through a sunbeam with the expression of a stockbroker watching a ticker he didn't trust.

Hamm: "You know what I don't understand? Weekends. What's the return on investment? The kid sleeps in, we get an extra two hours of nothing, and then it's exactly the same as a Tuesday except the cartoons are louder."

Mr. Potato Head: "You don't understand weekends because you're a piggy bank. Your whole life is about accumulation. You can't enjoy spending time."

Hamm: "Coming from a potato whose idea of a good time is making sure his ears are on the right way."

Mr. Potato Head: "They were on backwards one time. ONE TIME. And you bring it up every —"

Hamm: "— Every time it's funny. Which is every time."

Woody leaned back against the wall and let the sounds wash over him like a warm bath. The bickering. The Shakespeare. The clickety-clack of Trixie's typing. Rex's worried humming. Dolly's organizing. This was the soundtrack of a room that was alive — not with danger or adventure, but with the quiet, ordinary miracle of being together.

If you'd told me a year ago that this would feel like home, I'd have said you were crazier than a cat in a car wash. But here I am. And here they are. And I wouldn't trade a single one of them — not even Hamm on his worst day. — Woody, internal monologue
🤠 ⭐ 🤠

Buzz Lightyear walked over with the measured, deliberate stride of a man — a toy — a space ranger who had long since made peace with the distinction between those three words. He'd been doing his morning inspection of the room's perimeter, which he still called a "security sweep" even though the most dangerous thing he'd ever found was a rogue marble that had rolled under the dresser.

Buzz: "Perimeter is secure. No anomalies. Atmospheric conditions are stable. Humidity is... moderate."

Woody: "You can just say 'the room is fine,' Buzz."

Buzz: "I could. But thoroughness is the hallmark of a reliable operative."

Woody shook his head, but he was smiling. Some things about Buzz Lightyear never changed, and Woody had learned — slowly, over years of friendship that had started with jealousy and competition and had hardened into something unbreakable — that those unchanging things were features, not bugs. Buzz was disciplined. Buzz was loyal. Buzz took his responsibilities seriously, even the ones he'd invented for himself. And when the world went sideways, Buzz was the one you wanted standing next to you.

Buzz settled beside Woody against the baseboard, and for a long moment, neither of them spoke. They watched the room the way two old friends watch a sunset — not because there's anything to say, but because the watching itself is the conversation.

Woody: "You ever notice how quiet it's been lately?"

Buzz: "Define 'quiet.' Pricklepants alone generates approximately forty-seven decibels of unprompted soliloquy per hour."

Woody: "I don't mean the room. I mean... events. Nothing's happened. No new toys. No moves. No garage sales. No... you know. Adventures."

Buzz: "You say that like it's a bad thing."

Woody: "No! No, it's not bad. It's good. It's great, actually. Bonnie's happy. We're all together. Everyone's in one piece — well, except for the parts of Potato Head that are always somewhere they shouldn't be. It's just..."

He trailed off. Buzz waited. That was another thing about Buzz — he knew when to wait.

Woody: "I've been doing this a long time, Buzz. Taking care of toys, watching out for kids. And in my experience, the quiet stretches... they're the ones that come right before something big happens."

Buzz: "Or they're just quiet stretches. Not everything is foreshadowing, Woody."

Woody: "Says the space ranger who does a 'security sweep' of a seven-year-old's bedroom every morning."

Buzz: "...Touché."

Jessie dropped down from the shelf above them, landing with the effortless grace of a cowgirl who'd been swinging from things her entire existence. Her red braid bounced against her back, and her green eyes sparkled with the kind of energy that made Woody tired just looking at it.

Jessie: "Morning, boys! Beautiful day, isn't it? Sun's out, birds are singing, Pricklepants is doing the Scottish play — somebody should warn him about the curse —"

Woody: "He's doing Hamlet, actually."

Jessie: "Same difference. Point is — it's Saturday! Bonnie's gonna wake up any minute, and I've got a feeling today's gonna be a good one. I can feel it in my pull-string."

She clapped Buzz on the shoulder hard enough to make his chest plate rattle. Buzz straightened his posture instinctively — Jessie had that effect on him.

Buzz: "Your optimism is... admirable, Jessie."

Jessie: "Optimism? Nah. I'm just happy to be awake and not inside a storage box. Every morning I'm not in a box is a win. Everything else is just bonus rounds."

She said it lightly — a tossed-off joke, a grin, a flip of her braid. But Woody caught the flicker behind her eyes: a shadow that never fully left, no matter how many years passed, no matter how many mornings she woke up in a room full of friends instead of darkness and silence. Some scars didn't show on the outside. Some scars were stitched into the fabric.

🌟 ⭐ 🌟

On the far side of the room, Slinky Dog was attempting to teach Forky the concept of personal space. It was not going well.

Forky: "But why can't I sit on you?"

Slinky: "Because I'm not a chair, Forky. I'm a dog. Well — I'm a spring with a dog head and a dog tail. But the point stands."

Forky: "Slinky?"

Slinky: "Yep?"

Forky: "What's personal space?"

Slinky sighed — a long, patient sigh that rippled through his entire spring body like a gentle wave. He was the oldest toy in the room in terms of temperament if not manufacture date, and he had the steady, unhurried kindness of a country road that never rushed you to your destination. He'd been Woody's most faithful friend since the Andy days, and while Slinky didn't say much, what he did say was usually worth hearing.

Slinky: "Personal space is the invisible fence around a fella that says 'howdy, but not too close.' Like... imagine you're a piece of toast."

Forky: "Ohhh. I like toast."

Slinky: "Good. Now imagine another piece of toast comes along and just — plops right on top of you. No warning. No 'excuse me.' Just — smush."

Forky: "...That's a sandwich."

Slinky: "Exactly. And nobody wants to be a sandwich without consent, Forky. That's personal space."

Forky blinked his googly eyes with an expression of profound revelation, as though the entire architecture of social behavior had just snapped into focus through the metaphor of unwanted sandwich formation. He nodded slowly.

Forky: "So... I shouldn't sit on you because you'd be the bottom toast?"

Slinky: "...Close enough."

Woody watched the exchange from across the room and felt something warm settle in his chest — not warmth from the sunlight, but the deeper kind: the quiet pride of seeing your family be kind to one another, even when it was hard, even when it was ridiculous, even when it involved explaining sandwich consent to a repurposed spork.

This is what it's about, he thought. Not the adventures. Not the rescues. Not the big, dramatic moments that make you feel like a hero. It's the Saturday mornings. It's the toast metaphors. It's being here, together, and choosing this. Every single day.

🏠 ⭐ 🏠

The doorbell rang at 9:47 AM.

DING — DONG.

The sound cut through the room like a starting pistol. Every toy froze — not the fake, kid-is-coming freeze, but the instinctive, whole-body lock-up that happened when the outside world announced itself uninvited.

Woody's painted eyes flicked toward the bedroom door.

Through the walls, muffled but clear: footsteps on the stairs. Bonnie's mom's voice, cheerful and already in "hosting mode," the tone parents used when they were performing friendliness for other parents.

Bonnie's Mom: "Bonnie! Honey, come downstairs — Dennis from next door is here!"

A pause. Then the unmistakable sound of a seven-year-old being propelled from sleep to excitement in under three seconds: a thud (feet hitting the floor), a scramble (socks on hardwood), and a shriek that could have shattered crystal.

Bonnie: "DENNIS! Mom, is he bringing his toys?! He said he'd bring his new toys! He got them from the anime store last week and he said they're super cool and —"

The voice Dopplered away down the hallway, fading into the general commotion of a house shifting into playdate mode. Somewhere downstairs, a door opened. Grownup pleasantries were exchanged. A child's voice — higher, more tentative than Bonnie's — said "Hi."

Back in the bedroom, Woody turned to the group.

Woody: "Alright, everybody — we've got a playdate incoming. Standard protocol. You know the drill."

Hamm: "Standard protocol. Meaning: lie still and pretend we're not alive. Real challenging stuff."

Woody: "Hamm."

Hamm: "What? I'm just saying, it's not exactly a heist. We literally have to do nothing."

Woody: "The discipline of doing nothing well is what separates us from the toys that end up in the donation box. Positions, everyone."

They moved. Not quickly — there was time — but with the practiced efficiency of a team that had done this a thousand times. Trixie powered down the laptop and assumed her shelf position. Mr. Pricklepants abandoned Hamlet mid-soliloquy and returned to his display spot with a dignity that suggested he was merely taking an intermission. Dolly tucked herself beside the pillow. Buttercup yawned and didn't move because he was already in his spot. Rex leaned against the toy box at what he believed was a "natural" angle. Slinky coiled himself neatly. Forky stood very still, then toppled over, then stood up again, then toppled over again.

Forky: "Trash?"

Woody: "Not trash. Toy. We've talked about this."

Forky: "Right. Toy. Got it." He toppled over a third time. "...Toy who falls down a lot."

Buzz took his position on the shelf, arms at his sides, chin level, expression set to "heroic neutral" — his factory default face, the one that said I am a space ranger and I am ready for anything. Jessie curled herself beside a stuffed animal, her arm draped across it with careful casualness. Mr. Potato Head shoved his remaining ear into place just in time and went rigid.

And Woody — Sheriff Woody Pride — settled into his spot against the toy chest, hat tilted forward, pull-string coiled, smile painted on, looking exactly the way Bonnie had left him the night before.

The room went still.

Footsteps on the stairs. Two sets — one familiar, one new.

The door swung open, and Bonnie burst in like a small, pigtailed hurricane, dragging behind her a boy — eight years old, maybe, with a mop of dark hair and round glasses and a backpack that was almost bigger than he was.

Bonnie: "This is my room! These are all my toys — that's Woody, and that's Buzz Lightyear, and that's Jessie, she's a cowgirl, and that's Forky — he's special —"

Dennis: "Cool... you have a lot."

Bonnie: "You should see my mom's garage. There's even more in there. Did you bring YOUR toys? You SAID you would!"

Dennis nodded. He swung the oversized backpack off his shoulders and set it on the floor with a heavy thump that made the floorboards vibrate — and, if a toy had been paying very close attention, made something inside the backpack shift.

Dennis: "Yeah. I brought them. My dad got them from the anime store in the mall. They're from, like, six different shows. They're really cool. I'll show you —"

Bonnie's Mom: (from the hallway) "Bonnie, Dennis — it's so nice outside! Why don't you two go play in the backyard for a while? I'll make lemonade!"

The children exchanged a look — the universal kid look that said adults are interrupting, but free lemonade is free lemonade.

Bonnie: "Okay! But we're coming RIGHT back!" (to Dennis) "You can show me your toys after! Come on!"

They thundered out of the room like a two-person stampede. The door swung half-shut behind them. Footsteps receded. The back door slammed. Through the window, Woody could hear Bonnie already organizing some kind of yard game involving rules she was making up in real time.

Silence.

Woody held his position for the standard three-second grace period, then relaxed. All around the room, the other toys unfroze, stretching and sighing and returning to themselves.

Woody: "Alright. Headcount — everyone present and accounted for?"

He ran through the names the way he always did, a mental checklist refined over years: Buzz, Jessie, Bullseye, Rex, Hamm, Slinky, Mr. and Mrs. Potato Head, the Aliens, Dolly, Buttercup, Mr. Pricklepants, Trixie, Forky. All here. All fine.

Woody: "Good. We've got maybe thirty, forty minutes before they come back inside. Everyone stay loose but stay smart. Dennis's mom might come up to —"

He stopped.

Because the backpack was moving.

👀 ⭐ 👀

Not a lot. Not dramatically. Just a small, rhythmic vibration — like a heartbeat made of cardboard and plastic — coming from inside the half-open zipper of Dennis's backpack. The kind of movement that, if you weren't a toy who had spent his entire existence watching for the impossible, you'd dismiss as a trick of the light.

But Woody was a toy who had spent his entire existence watching for the impossible.

Woody: "...Buzz?"

Buzz: "I see it."

Jessie: "Is that backpack breathing?"

Hamm: "Backpacks don't breathe, Jessie. They don't have lungs. They're backpacks."

Jessie: "Then explain why it's moving, Mr. Science."

Hamm opened his mouth. Closed it. Looked at the backpack. Opened his mouth again.

Hamm: "...I have no explanation at this time."

The room went tense. That particular kind of tension that Woody knew too well — the crackling, electric silence that came right before everything changed. He'd felt it when Buzz first fell from the sky into Andy's room. He'd felt it in the darkness of Al's apartment. He'd felt it the day the moving truck pulled away from the house on Elm Street.

Something was about to happen. He could feel it in the stitching of his pull-string.

Slowly, cautiously, Woody approached the backpack. Buzz flanked him on the right. Jessie covered the left. Behind them, the rest of the toys formed a loose semicircle, a wall of painted eyes and held breath.

Inside the backpack, visible through the gap in the zipper: six small boxes. Each one was different — different sizes, different colors, different logos in a language that wasn't English. Japanese, Woody guessed, though he'd never seen Japanese before in his life. The boxes were decorated with vivid illustrations: a muscular figure in an orange uniform, a ninja with a headband, a boy in a straw hat, a swordsman in black robes, a kid in a green suit with wild hair, and a young man carrying a checkered pattern on his back.

And the boxes were shaking.

Not all at once. In sequence — one, then another, then another — as though whatever was inside was waking up, one by one, and testing the limits of its small cardboard prison.

Rex: "W-Woody? Should we be worried? Because I'm worried. I want to go on record as saying that I am currently, officially, deeply worried."

Mr. Potato Head: "Congratulations. You've been worried every day since 1995. This changes nothing."

Dolly: "Woody, what do we do?"

Woody stared at the shaking boxes. The colorful illustrations stared back at him — warriors and heroes from a world he didn't recognize, frozen in dramatic poses on glossy cardboard, their printed eyes full of an intensity he couldn't quite name.

He swallowed. A toy swallowing was, of course, a purely symbolic gesture — he had no throat, no saliva, no biological apparatus for swallowing. But sometimes the gesture was the point. Sometimes you needed to do the human thing, even when you weren't human, because it reminded you that courage wasn't about having no fear. It was about swallowing the fear and stepping forward anyway.

Woody: "Nobody panic. Whatever's in there... they're toys. They've gotta be. Dennis's toys. His new ones from the anime store."

Buzz: "Toys don't usually rattle their own boxes, Woody."

Woody: "Yeah, well — you tried to fly off a bed the first day you showed up. Toys do all kinds of things when they're new."

A box toppled out of the open backpack. Then another. Then four more, tumbling in a cascade of cardboard and cellophane, each one hitting the carpet with a muffled thwap. They lay in a scattered formation on the bedroom floor, six brightly colored parcels from a world none of them had ever seen.

The rattling stopped.

A silence fell over the room — deep, total, the kind of silence that doesn't just mean the absence of sound but the anticipation of it. Every toy in Bonnie's room stared at the six boxes on the floor. Rex whimpered softly. Forky leaned forward with the reckless curiosity of someone who didn't fully understand danger because he'd been a spork three months ago. Buttercup narrowed his plush eyes.

Buttercup: "Well. That's not ominous at all."

Then — one by one, starting with the orange box — the lids began to push open from the inside.

And Woody understood, with the absolute clarity of a toy who had seen impossible things and survived every single one of them, that the quiet stretch was over.

I've been doing this long enough to know when something's about to change everything. The air gets different. The light gets different. Even the dust motes stop moving, like the whole room is holding its breath. And right now? Right now, this room is holding its breath so hard I think the walls might crack. — Woody

Six boxes. Six lids. Six figures, stirring in the dark.

Woody tipped his hat down, squared his shoulders, and did what a sheriff does when the unknown rides into town.

He stood his ground.
