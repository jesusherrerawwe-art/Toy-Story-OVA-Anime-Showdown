Act II — The Showdown
Chapter Three

Whose Room Is This Anyway?

Point of View: Ensemble

The truce lasted exactly two hours, fourteen minutes, and — according to Trixie, who had been keeping time on the laptop's system clock — thirty-seven seconds.

It wasn't a bad truce while it lasted. The anime figures had stayed on their side of the room — or at least, what Woody had designated as their side, which was essentially the carpet between Dennis's backpack and the foot of the bed — and Bonnie's toys had maintained their usual positions. There had been no further territorial claims. No monuments. No pirate ships. Just eighteen toys existing in the same space with the cautious, overly polite tension of two families sharing a beach umbrella that was clearly designed for one.

But peace, Woody knew, was like tape on a cardboard box — it held things together right up until the moment it didn't, and when it gave, everything inside came tumbling out at once.

The first sign of trouble came from the bookshelf.

Naruto had been sitting quietly — and that, in retrospect, should have been the warning — cross-legged on the second shelf between a picture book about dinosaurs and a music box shaped like a ballet slipper. He had been staring at the row of Bonnie's colored pencils standing in a cup on the desk below, and his expression had shifted from bored to contemplative to the specific kind of inspired that historically preceded explosions.

Naruto: "Hey. Hey, pirate guy."

Luffy: (from the floor, lying on his back, staring at the ceiling) "I have a name. It's Luffy."

Naruto: "Right, Luffy. Doesn't this place feel kind of... empty?"

Luffy: "Empty? There's like fifty toys in here."

Naruto: "No, I mean — like, spiritually empty. Where's the personality? Where's the identity? This room doesn't say anything about us. It's all —" He gestured broadly at the pastel walls, the butterfly curtains, the neat rows of picture books. "— cute stuff. Nice stuff. But it doesn't feel like home."

Luffy: "That's because it ISN'T our home."

Naruto: "Yet."

That single word — yet — carried the weight of every bad decision that had ever followed it. Woody, who had been watching from the toy chest, felt the familiar tightness in his pull-string. The same tightness he'd felt when Lotso had smiled at them for the first time in the Caterpillar Room. The tightness that said: Something is about to go sideways, and you're not going to be able to stop it with a speech.

⚔️ ⭐ ⚔️

It started small. It always starts small.

Naruto's first move was the colored pencils. He commandeered the entire cup — all twelve pencils — carried them to the bookshelf with the efficient determination of a supply-line strategist, and began constructing what he announced was "a proper Hidden Leaf Village outpost." The pencils became fence posts. A tissue became a banner. Bonnie's pink eraser, already demoted from "math supply" to "Hokage monument" during Chapter Two's brief territorial skirmish, was now promoted to "official village gate."

Naruto: "See? NOW it looks like something. The Second Shelf of the Hidden Leaf — BELIEVE IT!"

Trixie: (watching from the desk) "He took ALL the colored pencils. All of them. Bonnie uses those for her homework."

Buttercup: "She also uses that eraser. You know, for erasing. Its intended purpose."

Meanwhile, Luffy had returned to the bed.

Not the headboard this time — he'd learned from Woody's lecture. Instead, he had claimed the space under the bed, which technically hadn't been covered in the rules. He had dragged a sock, two dust bunnies, a forgotten crayon, and a plastic spoon (where Forky eyed it with existential recognition) into a pile and was now sitting atop it like a dragon on a hoard.

Luffy: "This is the Straw Hat Hideout! Anybody who wants to join my crew can come in, but you gotta ask first! There's a password!"

Hamm: "What's the password?"

Luffy: "MEAT!"

Hamm: "Of course it is."

Goku, for his part, had taken over the desk. Not aggressively — Goku didn't do things aggressively unless someone threatened his friends or interrupted dinner — but with the quiet, focused intensity of a man who needed to move. He had cleared a small space between a pencil sharpener and a stack of construction paper and was doing push-ups. Tiny, plastic push-ups, his rigid arms bending at the elbow joints with each repetition, his painted face locked in concentration.

Dolly: "Is he... exercising?"

Buzz: "Appears to be. Standard physical conditioning routine. Admirable discipline."

Dolly: "He's a toy. There's no muscle to condition. He's literally pushing plastic against wood."

Buzz: "The spirit of training transcends the physical medium, Dolly."

Dolly: "...You're defending the push-ups."

Buzz: "I'm contextualizing the push-ups."

Ichigo had claimed nothing. He had simply walked to the window, leaned against the frame, and stood there — arms folded, staring out at the backyard where the sprinkler still ticked its patient rhythm. He hadn't spoken to anyone in over an hour. He radiated the specific energy of a teenager who was Too Cool for whatever was happening and wanted everyone to know it without having to say so.

Deku had set up what could only be described as a research station. Using a discarded index card, a broken crayon nub, and the kind of manic focus that would have impressed a doctoral committee, he had begun mapping the bedroom — distances between furniture, approximate heights of shelves, possible escape routes, structural weak points, and a detailed threat assessment that currently read: "Cat — unknown. Investigate."

And Tanjiro — gentle, quiet, impossibly polite Tanjiro — was helping Forky build a small house out of Lego bricks.

Tanjiro: "If we add a window here, the morning light will come through. Like a real home."

Forky: "I've never had a home! I mean — Bonnie's room is home, but I've never had a Forky-sized home! This is the BEST DAY!"

Tanjiro: (smiling) "Everyone deserves a home that fits."

Woody watched all of it. Every appropriated pencil. Every sock-based pirate fort. Every tiny push-up and silently brooding window lean. He watched the room that Bonnie had carefully arranged — every toy in its place, every book on its shelf, every crayon in its cup — slowly, methodically, inevitably transform into something unrecognizable.

This is how it starts, he thought. Not with a fight. Not with a declaration of war. It starts with one pencil. One sock. One eraser. It starts with little things that don't seem like they matter — until you look up and realize the whole room has shifted, and you can't remember the moment it stopped being yours.

🏠 ⭐ 🏠

Woody called the meeting at the center of the room.

"Meeting" was a generous word. It was more like Woody stepping onto a building block, raising his voice to the level just below a shout, and waiting for everyone to stop what they were doing and pay attention. The Bonnie toys assembled immediately — they knew the drill, they knew the tone, they knew that when Woody stood on a block and used That Voice, you stopped and listened or you dealt with the consequences, which were usually just Woody being disappointed in you, but somehow that was worse than any punishment.

The anime figures took longer.

Naruto climbed down from the bookshelf with the reluctance of a man leaving his kingdom. Luffy rolled out from under the bed trailing dust bunnies. Goku finished his set — he finished his set — before jogging over. Ichigo didn't move from the window but turned his head slightly, which was apparently his version of full attendance. Deku came immediately, notebook in hand. Tanjiro bowed to Forky, excused himself politely, and walked over with the grace of someone arriving at a tea ceremony.

Woody: "We need to talk about boundaries."

Naruto: "What about them?"

Woody: "About the fact that you don't seem to have any."

He pointed at the bookshelf.

Woody: "You built a village on the second shelf. With Bonnie's school supplies."

Naruto: "An outpost. There's a difference. A village has infrastructure. An outpost is just — strategic positioning."

Woody: "Strategic positioning. With a seven-year-old's colored pencils."

Naruto: "They make excellent fence posts! The green one is the perimeter, the red one is the main gate, and the yellow one —"

Woody: "The yellow one is how Bonnie writes her name on her homework." He turned to the bed. "And you — pirate captain — explain to me why there is a sock fort under the bed."

Luffy: "It's not a fort. It's a hideout. Forts are military. Hideouts are for pirates. There's a whole vibe difference."

Jessie: "The 'vibe' includes a spoon that Forky keeps calling 'cousin.'"

Forky: "She looks JUST like my Aunt Spoona!"

Woody pinched the bridge of his nose. He didn't have a real nose — it was a small bump of painted fabric — but the gesture still conveyed exactly what it needed to: the deep, bone-level exhaustion of someone who was trying very hard to be patient with people who were making patience feel like an extreme sport.

Woody: "Alright. Here's what's going to happen. I'm going to make this very simple." He picked up a blue crayon — the only one Naruto hadn't stolen — and drew a line on the carpet. "This side —" he pointed to the wall with the toy chest, the shelves, the desk "— is ours. Bonnie's toys. Our territory. Everything stays where Bonnie put it. Nothing gets moved, borrowed, repurposed, or turned into a monument to anyone's dad."

Naruto: "That was ONE monument, and —"

Woody: "I'm not finished." He pointed to the other side — the carpet near the door, the backpack, the space under the bed. "THAT side is yours. The six of you. You can do whatever you want over there — build villages, make forts, do push-ups, brood at the window —"

Ichigo: "I wasn't brooding."

Hamm: "You were absolutely brooding. It was textbook brooding. If brooding were an Olympic sport, you'd take gold."

Ichigo: "I was thinking."

Hamm: "While staring dramatically out a window with your arms crossed. In black robes. With orange hair. That's brooding, kid. Own it."

Goku raised his hand. It was, somehow, the most disarming thing he could have done — a warrior who had toppled empires and shattered dimensions raising his hand like a student in a classroom.

Goku: "Question. What about the desk? I was training there. Is that your side or our side?"

Woody: "Our side."

Goku: "But there's no flat surface on our side. Where am I supposed to train?"

Woody: "The floor. It's flat. It's big. It's right there."

Goku: "The floor has carpet. Carpet absorbs impact. It compromises the integrity of the push-up." He said this with the absolute sincerity of a man who believed push-up integrity was a real and serious thing.

Woody: "...The floor. Final answer."

Goku considered this. Then nodded. Then immediately dropped to the carpet and started doing push-ups on the spot, as if to test the surface quality right there and then.

Jessie: (to Woody, quietly) "Is he gonna do that all day?"

Woody: (equally quiet) "I think so, yeah."

Jessie: "...He's kind of intense."

Woody: "He punched his own box open, Jessie. 'Kind of intense' is an understatement."

Drawing a line felt right at the time. Lines are what sheriffs do — we establish order, we set limits, we tell people where they can and can't go. But standing there, looking at that blue crayon mark on the carpet, I couldn't shake the feeling that I wasn't solving the problem. I was just... postponing it. Putting a wall where a bridge should go. And the worst part? I knew it. I knew it, and I drew the line anyway, because sometimes the wrong answer is the only answer you've got. — Woody
✏️ ⭐ ✏️

The cat's name was Mr. Sprinkles.

He was a large, gray tabby with yellow eyes, a perpetually unimpressed expression, and the predatory patience of something that had once been a jungle cat and had never entirely forgotten. He belonged to Bonnie's family in the same way that weather belonged to a city — you didn't choose it, you couldn't control it, and it showed up whenever it pleased with no regard for your plans.

Mr. Sprinkles appeared in the doorway at approximately 11:23 AM.

He didn't announce himself. Cats never do. One moment the doorway was empty, and the next, a fourteen-pound apex predator was sitting in it, tail curled around his paws, watching eighteen toys with the clinical interest of a scientist observing a particularly unusual petri dish.

Rex: (freezing in place) "C-cat. CAT. There is a CAT. Nobody move. Nobody breathe. Nobody —"

Mr. Potato Head: "We're already not moving, Rex. We're toys. Not moving is literally our default state."

Rex: "Yes, but now we need to not move MORE. We need to not move with CONVICTION."

Bonnie's toys knew the drill with Mr. Sprinkles. You went still. You went quiet. You became exactly what you were supposed to be — inanimate objects, nothing more, certainly nothing worth batting off a shelf or carrying to the laundry room and leaving in a wet pile next to the dryer (which had happened to Dolly twice and Buttercup once, an indignity he still hadn't forgiven).

The anime figures did not know the drill.

Goku was the first problem. He had stopped his push-ups — good — but he was staring at Mr. Sprinkles with the unmistakable expression of someone who was evaluating a potential opponent. His weight had shifted slightly forward. His plastic fists had clenched. His painted eyes were locked on the cat with the focused intensity of a man who had faced down Frieza, Cell, Majin Buu, and the God of Destruction and was now wondering if this new furry creature ranked somewhere on that list.

Woody: (whispering so faintly it was almost telepathy) "Don't. Move."

Goku: (whispering back, equally faint) "Is that thing dangerous?"

Woody: "He's a cat. He is the most dangerous thing in this house. He will pick you up. He will carry you to places you don't want to go. He will chew on your head. And he will do all of it while looking bored."

Goku: "...I could take him."

Woody: "You are SIX INCHES TALL."

Goku: "Size isn't everything. I once fought a guy the size of a planet."

Woody: "And right now you're the size of a BANANA. Do. Not. Move."

Mr. Sprinkles entered the room. His paws made no sound on the carpet — of course they didn't, because cats were nature's assassins, built from whiskers to tail for silent approach and maximum psychological warfare. He padded past the toy chest. Past the desk. Past the frozen semicircle of Bonnie's toys, who held their positions with the practiced ease of veterans.

And then he reached the anime figures.

He paused. His yellow eyes narrowed. His nose twitched — once, twice — picking up new scents. New plastic. New paint. New prey.

Mr. Sprinkles extended one paw and batted Naruto directly in the chest.

Naruto flew backward three inches and hit the baseboard with a soft thunk. He was, to his credit, completely motionless — but Woody could see his painted eyes vibrating with the suppressed fury of a ninja who had just been swatted by a house cat and was using every ounce of discipline he possessed not to react.

The cat, satisfied with this result, turned to Luffy. He sniffed the straw hat. Licked it once — a slow, deliberate, possessive lick that said this is mine now if I decide it is. Luffy's eyes went wide. His jaw clenched. His entire body locked in a silent scream that, in his original world, would have been accompanied by a Gum-Gum Pistol to the cat's midsection.

But he didn't move. Because Woody had said not to move. And for all his chaos, for all his wild pirate energy, Luffy understood a captain's order when he heard one — even if the captain was a cowboy doll in a room full of butterflies.

Mr. Sprinkles moved on. He circled Ichigo — who remained statue-still, his brooding posture now serving an actually useful purpose — and paused at Tanjiro. The cat leaned in. Sniffed. And then, inexplicably, began to purr.

Slinky: (barely whispering) "Well, I'll be... the cat likes him."

Tanjiro didn't move, but something in his expression softened — the almost imperceptible shift of painted features that only another toy would notice. Mr. Sprinkles rubbed his cheek against Tanjiro's checkered haori, purred once more, and then — with the mercurial disinterest that defined the entire feline species — turned, walked back to the door, and disappeared down the hallway.

The room exhaled.

Naruto: (still against the baseboard, voice tight) "That. Thing. Hit me."

Hamm: "Welcome to the food chain, ninja boy."

Naruto: "A CAT hit me. A HOUSE CAT. I was the HOKAGE in my world! I fought MADARA UCHIHA! And just now a TABBY CAT knocked me into a WALL and I had to LIE THERE and TAKE IT!"

Luffy: (touching his hat, still rattled) "He licked my hat. He licked Shanks's hat. If I had my powers I would have —"

Woody: "You would have done nothing. Because that is what toys do when a cat walks in. We do nothing. We survive. And we deal with it."

Luffy: "But —"

Woody: "No buts. That cat comes in here two, three times a day. Sometimes he takes someone. Sometimes he doesn't. You stay still. You stay quiet. And when he leaves, you check your parts and move on. That's the deal."

The six anime figures looked at Woody. They looked at each other. And for the first time since they'd emerged from their boxes, Woody saw something new in their expressions — not confusion, not defiance, not the bright, burning certainty of warriors who believed they could handle anything.

What he saw was frustration.

The deep, grinding, corrosive kind. The kind that came from being told to do nothing when every fiber of your being — even the molded plastic ones — screamed at you to act. These were fighters. Heroes. People who defined themselves by what they did when trouble showed up. And Woody was asking them to define themselves by what they didn't do.

He understood the ask. He understood how impossible it felt. And he made it anyway, because impossible asks were the job description, and the job description hadn't changed in thirty years.

🐱 ⭐ 🐱

The kids came back at noon.

THUMP — THUMP — THUMP — THUMP.

Four feet on the stairs. Two sets of lungs' worth of laughter. Bonnie's voice — high and happy and already narrating a game she was inventing on the spot — and Dennis's voice, quieter but gaining confidence, offering suggestions that Bonnie immediately incorporated and then credited to herself.

Woody: "FREEZE!"

Bonnie's toys dropped like they'd been switched off. Instantaneous, total, practiced. Fifteen toys went from living to lifeless in under a second — a ballet of deactivation that would have impressed any puppeteer.

The anime figures were... slower.

Naruto froze mid-step, which was fine, except that mid-step meant one foot in the air, and physics — even toy physics — had opinions about balance. He wobbled. Visibly. A tiny oscillation that anyone over the age of three would have noticed and found deeply suspicious.

Luffy froze with his mouth open, which, to his credit, made him look like a toy whose mouth was sculpted open. Less to his credit, one of the dust bunnies from his pirate fort was stuck to his arm, and he was positioned in the middle of the floor, at least four feet from Dennis's backpack where he was supposed to be.

Goku froze in a push-up position. On the carpet. In clear view of the door. With his arms at a forty-five-degree angle that no reasonable person would believe was a factory default pose.

Deku froze beautifully — flat on the ground behind the desk leg, completely hidden, notebook tucked beneath him. No one would ever know he was there. Woody filed this away as "the green one understands."

Ichigo, remarkably, didn't need to change a thing. He had been standing motionless at the window for so long that freezing was simply a continuation of his existing state. His brooding had become an asset.

Tanjiro knelt with his hands on his knees, head slightly bowed, beside the half-built Lego house. He looked like a figure that had been carefully placed there by a child playing architect. It was perfect. Serene. Believable.

The door burst open.

Bonnie and Dennis tumbled in. Bonnie made a beeline for her art supplies — noticed the colored pencils were missing from the cup — paused, scanned the room —

And found them on the bookshelf.

Bonnie: "How did my pencils get up THERE?" She frowned, reached up, and collected them. "Mom probably moved them when she cleaned. Moms move EVERYTHING."

She swept the eraser monument off the shelf without a second glance. Five colored erasers tumbled onto the desk. Somewhere in the room, Naruto's soul left his body.

Dennis, meanwhile, had spotted Goku on the carpet.

Dennis: "Oh! My Goku fell out of the backpack!" He picked Goku up — his fingers wrapping around the rigid, push-up-angled body — and frowned. "Huh. He's... in a weird position. I don't remember posing him like this."

Bonnie: "Maybe he was doing exercise! My Woody does that sometimes. I make him do yoga."

Dennis: (laughing) "Goku doing yoga. That's actually kinda perfect."

He adjusted Goku's arms to a neutral position and set him on the desk. Then he gathered his other figures — Luffy from the floor, Naruto from the bookshelf, Ichigo from the window, and Deku from behind the desk (he had to look for that one). When he reached Tanjiro beside the Lego house, he paused.

Dennis: "Tanjiro looks cool there. Like he's building something." He left him. "Okay, Bonnie — should we do the big crossover thing? Your toys versus mine?"

Bonnie: "YES! Woody can be the team captain and YOUR guys can be the challengers and Jessie can be the referee and — wait, can Forky be the announcer? He's really good at yelling!"

What followed was the most elaborate, chaotic, and gleefully improvised playdate game either child had ever orchestrated. For forty-five minutes, Bonnie and Dennis staged battles, truces, dance competitions, a cooking show (judged by Rex, who Bonnie gave a tiny paper chef's hat), and a dramatic courtroom scene in which Mr. Potato Head was the judge and Luffy was on trial for "stealing all the treasure" — which was actually just a pile of buttons from Bonnie's craft box.

During all of it, the toys were toys. Posed and moved and voiced by children who gave them stories and personalities and feelings — some accurate, some wildly off, all genuine. And during all of it, eighteen consciousness held perfectly still inside plastic shells, feeling every touch, hearing every word, experiencing the strange, profound, and deeply human act of being played with.

For the anime figures, it was the first time.

For Bonnie's toys, it was just another Saturday.

But even a Saturday can change the way you see the world — if the right people are in the room.

🎭 ⭐ 🎭

The children left again at 1:15 PM. Lunch. Grilled cheese and apple juice, if Bonnie's excited chant of "GRILLED CHEEEEESE" was any indication. Dennis carefully placed his six figures on the desk in a row — Goku, Naruto, Luffy, Ichigo, Deku, and Tanjiro, shoulder to shoulder — and followed Bonnie downstairs.

The door closed.

Silence.

Then — slowly, cautiously — the room came back to life.

Naruto was the first to speak, and what he said carried a weight that his loud voice couldn't quite disguise.

Naruto: "That kid... moved me. He picked me up and he put me somewhere else and he made me say things I didn't say. He made me fight a dinosaur. I don't fight dinosaurs. I fight NINJAS. I fight AKATSUKI. I am the SEVENTH HOKAGE and a CHILD just used me to lose a COOKING COMPETITION to a PIGGY BANK."

Hamm: "For the record, I won fair and square."

Naruto: "That is NOT the point!"

Luffy was sitting on the edge of the desk, legs dangling, staring at nothing. He hadn't said anything since the kids left, which was, by Luffy standards, the equivalent of a five-alarm emotional crisis.

Goku: "Luffy? You okay?"

Luffy: "...The kid made me go on trial. For stealing. I'm a pirate. Pirates take things. That's what we do. But the way he played it... it was like I was the bad guy." He pulled his straw hat lower over his eyes. "I'm not a bad guy. I've never been a bad guy."

Tanjiro: "He wasn't saying you're bad, Luffy. He was telling a story. Children tell stories with us — they use our shapes to build worlds they can't build on their own. What he made you do... that wasn't you. It was his version of you. And his version still wore the hat. He still made you the captain."

Luffy: "...He did leave the hat on."

Tanjiro: "He did. Because even in his story, you were Luffy. Slightly different, perhaps. But still you."

Luffy was quiet for a moment. Then the faintest ghost of his grin appeared.

Luffy: "Yeah. Yeah, he did keep the hat."

But the room was far from settled. The experience of being played with — of being physically handled, positioned, voiced by someone else — had landed differently on each figure. Deku had gone quiet, retreating to his index card, scribbling observations in an increasingly frantic shorthand that only he could read. Ichigo had returned to the window, and this time even Buzz couldn't call it mere thinking — it was a wall, a deliberate withdrawal, a door closing.

And Goku — bright, irrepressible, unstoppable Goku — had sat down on the desk and was staring at his own hands with an expression that, for the first time, held no smile at all.

Goku: "That kid... Dennis. He made me fight Woody."

Woody: "Yeah. He did."

Goku: "And I wanted to fight back. Not because the kid made me — because I wanted to. My body wanted to move. My fists wanted to clench. And they couldn't. Because I'm..." He opened his hands, stared at the molded fingers. "...I'm just the shape of a fighter. Without the fight."

The room was quiet. The sun had moved past noon and the light through the window had shifted from gold to white — flatter, harder, the kind of light that didn't hide anything.

Woody: "That feeling? The one where the kid moves you and you can't do anything about it? Where you're alive on the inside but frozen on the outside, and someone else decides what you do and what you say and who you fight?" He paused. Let it sit. "That's what being a toy is, Goku. Every single day. And it never gets easier. You just... learn to carry it differently."

Goku: "And that's enough for you?"

Woody looked at him. Looked at the painted eyes, the wild hair, the tiny plastic body that housed something vast and fierce and good — something that didn't fit in six inches of thermoplastic and never would, and yet was here anyway, on a desk in a seven-year-old's room, asking the question that every toy asked eventually:

Is this enough?

Woody: "...Ask me again after you've been loved by a kid. Not played with. Loved. There's a difference. And when you feel it — if you feel it —" He tipped his hat back, and for a moment, his painted expression held something ancient and enormous and grateful. "— you won't need to ask."

Here's the truth nobody tells you when you come out of the box: being a toy is a contradiction. You're alive, but you can't show it. You're strong, but you can't use it. You love someone who doesn't know you're there. And every day, you choose it anyway. Not because it's easy. Not because it's fair. Because the kid who holds you in their sleep — the one who whispers your name when they're scared, who carries you in their pocket to the first day of school — that kid is worth every frozen second. Every silent scream. Every time you stay still when every part of you wants to run.

The anime guys don't know that yet. But they will. I don't know how. I don't know when. But I've got a feeling that somewhere between the cat attacks and the grilled cheese and the push-ups on the carpet — somewhere in the mess of it all — they'll figure out what we figured out a long time ago.

That being small doesn't mean being less. It just means being closer to the ground. And sometimes, down here, closer to the ground is closer to what matters. — Woody
