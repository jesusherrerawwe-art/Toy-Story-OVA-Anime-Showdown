Act II — The Showdown
Chapter Four

The Cracks in the Armor

Point of View: Deku & Buzz

The index card was full.

Both sides. Every margin. Every corner. Deku had written so small that the letters blurred into one another like a city seen from space — dense, intricate, and completely illegible to anyone who wasn't him. He held the card at arm's length, squinting at his own handwriting, and felt something he hadn't felt since the day he'd learned he was born without a Quirk:

The cold, clinical certainty that the numbers didn't lie.

He had started the research as a coping mechanism. That's what Deku did when the world stopped making sense — he studied it until it did. While the others had reacted to being played with through anger or silence or existential hat-gazing, Deku had retreated behind the desk leg, pulled out his broken crayon nub, and started testing hypotheses.

Hypothesis One: Their powers were suppressed by the environment. Some kind of dampening field, like the Quirk-Erasing bullets Overhaul had used. If the room itself was the variable, then leaving the room should restore their abilities.

He'd tested this by crawling to the doorway when no one was looking — one foot in the bedroom, one foot in the hallway. He'd tried to activate One For All at five percent. Nothing. He'd tried Full Cowling. Nothing. He'd flexed every joint, strained every connection point, reached for the spark that had lived in his bones since All Might had passed it to him.

There was no spark. There were no bones. There were injection-molded joint sockets and a hollow torso cavity where the factory had saved on material costs.

Hypothesis One: Rejected.

Hypothesis Two: Their powers required a minimum body mass to function. They were six inches tall. Perhaps ki, chakra, Haki, Reiatsu, One For All, and Breathing Techniques all had a threshold — a minimum scale below which the energy couldn't manifest. Like how a fire couldn't burn without enough oxygen.

He'd calculated their approximate mass. Factored in the density of ABS thermoplastic (1.05 g/cm³, according to the label on Trixie's packaging that he'd found in the recycling bin next to the desk). Compared it to the known mass of a human body. Cross-referenced with the energy output of a five-percent Full Cowling activation.

The numbers were devastating.

Even if their powers could scale down, the energy-to-mass ratio at six inches was so catastrophically unbalanced that activating any ability would either do nothing or — theoretically — vaporize them. There was no middle ground. No gentle application. They were either powerless or dust.

Hypothesis Two: Rejected.

Hypothesis Three was the one he hadn't wanted to write down. The one he'd been circling around since the first moment he'd tried to clench his fist and felt nothing but the smooth, pre-molded curve of plastic fingers that had never been designed to clench at all.

Hypothesis Three: Their powers didn't work because their powers had never existed. Not here. Not in these bodies. What they remembered — the battles, the training, the Quirks and ki and chakra — were memories encoded in consciousness that now inhabited objects that had been manufactured to represent those memories, but had never actually contained them. They were portraits of warriors. Not warriors. The painting of a fire doesn't burn.

Deku stared at the words. Read them again. Read them a third time.

And then he set the index card down, leaned his back against the desk leg, and did something he hadn't done since the entrance exam for U.A. — something he'd thought he'd outgrown, something he'd thought the scars on his hands and the fire in his heart had burned away forever.

He cried.

Not loudly. Not the way Naruto cried — with the full-throated, unapologetic force of someone who believed emotions should be felt at maximum volume. Deku cried the way analysts cry: quietly, precisely, with the terrible efficiency of someone who understood exactly what had been lost and could calculate its value to fourteen decimal places.

I've always believed that heroes are defined by what they do, not what they have. All Might taught me that. He gave away One For All when his body couldn't hold it anymore, and he was still a hero — still the Symbol of Peace — even as a skeleton in an oversized suit. But he was still human. He still had hands that could hold things and feet that could run and a voice that could reach people. I have... molded plastic. A hollow chest. Joints that click when I move. I have the memory of being a hero inside a body that was built to be held by one. — Deku
💔 ⭐ 💔

Ichigo found him first.

Or rather, Ichigo had been on his way to the window — his window, the one he'd claimed through the universal squatter's rights of consistent brooding — when he passed the desk and heard the sound. A small sound. The kind of sound that plastic shouldn't be able to make but somehow did when the consciousness inside it was in enough pain.

He stopped. Looked down. Saw Deku pressed against the desk leg with his knees drawn up and his index card on the ground beside him, and understood immediately.

Ichigo: "You figured it out."

It wasn't a question.

Deku: (wiping his eyes with the back of his hand, which accomplished nothing because plastic didn't absorb tears and tears didn't exist on a face that couldn't produce them) "The numbers... Ichigo, the numbers are —"

Ichigo: "Definitive. I know." He sat down. Not gently — Ichigo didn't do things gently — but with the controlled weight of someone who had decided to be present. "I figured it out in the box. Before any of us opened our eyes. The second I felt my body — the way the joints locked, the way the plastic hummed instead of a heartbeat — I knew."

Deku: "You KNEW? Since the beginning? And you didn't say anything?"

Ichigo: "What was I supposed to say? 'Hey, guys, good news — we're all action figures with fake memories and no powers, also there's a cat'? Yeah, that would've gone over great with the guy who thinks he's Hokage and the rubber man claiming a seven-year-old's bed."

Deku: "But the others — they deserve to know —"

Ichigo: "They do know. All of them. They just haven't admitted it yet." He leaned back against the desk. "Goku stopped smiling after the kid played with him. Have you noticed that? Goku. The guy who smiles at everything. The guy who grinned when he found out he had no powers. After that kid moved him like a puppet... the smile stopped. That's not ignorance. That's someone who knows the truth and is trying to decide what to do with it."

Deku was quiet. He looked at his index card. At the meticulous calculations, the careful hypotheses, the three rejected possibilities and the one devastating certainty. All that work. All that analysis. And Ichigo had known since the box.

Deku: "Then... what do we do?"

Ichigo: "That's the question, isn't it?" He stared at the ceiling — at the glow-in-the-dark star stickers Bonnie had placed in a pattern that didn't match any real constellation but seemed to match something in a seven-year-old's imagination. "Back in my world — the real one, or whatever we're calling it — when I lost my powers, I didn't stop being me. I was still Ichigo. Still the same angry kid from Karakura Town who got into fights and yelled at his dad and couldn't keep his spiritual pressure under control. The powers were gone, but I wasn't."

Deku: "But that's different. You were still human. You still had a body that was yours. This —" He held up his hand, turned it in the fading afternoon light. "— this isn't mine. This was built in a factory. Molded from a pellet. Painted by a machine. This hand has never held anything. It was designed to look like it could."

Ichigo looked at Deku's hand. Small, green-gloved, rigid. The fingers curved slightly inward in a fist that would never close and never open, frozen in a sculptor's approximation of readiness.

Ichigo: "Yeah. It was." Pause. "And right now, that hand just wiped tears off a face that can't cry, held a crayon it was never designed to hold, and wrote calculations on a piece of paper that would impress a physicist. So maybe — maybe — what it was designed for isn't the whole story."

The silence that followed was the kind that holds things — not empty, but full. The kind of silence that happens when two people who are very good at thinking arrive at the same thought at the same time and don't need to say it out loud.

What they were was undeniable. What they could be was still open.

🌑 ⭐ 🌑

Buzz Lightyear had been listening.

Not eavesdropping — that implied secrecy, and Buzz didn't trade in secrets. He had been on the shelf above the desk, performing a routine inventory of the art supplies Bonnie kept in the mason jar (seven crayons, four markers, two dried-out glue sticks, and a pencil with a chewed eraser), when Deku's quiet distress had reached him. He had stopped counting. He had listened. And he had recognized, with the clarity of a man who had walked this exact road, every single word.

He climbed down.

Ichigo saw him first and tensed — the automatic response of a fighter who still registered approaching footsteps as potential threats, even in a bedroom. But Buzz's body language was unmistakable: shoulders relaxed, hands visible, pace measured. He wasn't approaching as a sheriff's deputy or a room authority. He was approaching as someone who had something to give.

Buzz: "May I?"

He gestured to the space beside Deku. Deku nodded. Buzz sat down — carefully, deliberately, the way he did everything — and for a long moment, he didn't speak. He just sat. Let the silence do its work. Let Deku feel the presence of someone who wasn't going to ask him to stop feeling what he was feeling.

Then:

Buzz: "I need to tell you something. Both of you. It's not a comfortable story, and I don't tell it often. But I think... I think you need to hear it right now more than I need to keep it to myself."

Ichigo: "The box story. The day you found out."

Buzz: "Not just that. The whole thing. The part nobody talks about — the part that came after."

He straightened. Took a breath — or the toy equivalent of a breath, which was a small recalibration of posture and intention. And then Buzz Lightyear, Space Ranger (Retired), told his story.

Buzz: "When I came out of my box in Andy's room, I believed everything on the packaging. Every word. I was Buzz Lightyear of Star Command. I had a laser — a real laser. Wings that flew. A mission log that was transmitting to a command center that was waiting for my report. The whole world made sense. I was a hero in a strange land, and all I had to do was complete my mission and go home."

He paused. The afternoon light had shifted again — longer now, amber, casting thin shadows across the carpet.

Buzz: "Woody tried to tell me. He tried a dozen different ways. And I fought him. Not just with words — I mean I physically fought him, because admitting he was right would have meant admitting that everything I believed about myself was a lie. And I wasn't ready. I wasn't..." He faltered, and for the briefest moment, the Space Ranger's composure cracked. "I wasn't strong enough."

Deku: (quietly) "What changed?"

Buzz: "A window. Sid's house — the neighbor kid, the one who... modified toys. I tried to fly. Not to prove a point. Not to impress anyone. I tried to fly because I needed it to be true. I stood on the top of the staircase railing and I spread my wings and I jumped."

Silence.

Buzz: "I didn't fly. I fell. Down the stairs. Hit the floor. And my arm..." He unconsciously touched his left forearm, the joint where the original break had been repaired so long ago that only he and Woody knew the seam was there. "My arm broke off. Cleanly. Just — separated from my body. And I lay there on the floor of a house that belonged to a child who destroyed toys for fun, with one arm and no wings and no mission and no Star Command, and I looked up at the ceiling and I thought:"

He stopped again. This time the pause was longer.

Buzz: "'I'm just a toy. A stupid, little, insignificant toy.'"

Deku's breath caught — or whatever happened inside a toy's chest when the equivalent of a breath tried to form and found nowhere to go.

Buzz: "And then something worse happened. Sid's sister found me. Hannah. She picked me up and she dressed me in a hat and an apron and she sat me at a tiny table and she poured me imaginary tea and she called me... Mrs. Nesbitt."

Ichigo: (blinking) "...Mrs. Nesbitt?"

Buzz: "Mrs. Nesbitt. And do you know what I did? I played along. I sat at that table and I drank the imaginary tea and I became Mrs. Nesbitt, because being Mrs. Nesbitt was easier than being Buzz Lightyear — the Buzz Lightyear who couldn't fly and never could and had believed a lie his entire existence." He shook his head. "That was rock bottom. That was the floor beneath the floor. The place you go when even the lies stop working and you haven't figured out what the truth looks like yet."

The room had gone very quiet. Not just around the desk — the whole room. Woody, who had been across the room pretending not to listen, had stopped pretending. Jessie stood motionless by the toy chest, one hand on her braid. Even Naruto, who had been rebuilding his pencil outpost after Bonnie's devastating disassembly, had gone still.

Deku: "How did you... come back from that?"

Buzz: "Woody." Simple. Immediate. No hesitation. "Woody came and found me at the tea party. And I was a mess — delirious, broken, one arm missing, wearing a hat with daisies on it. And he didn't fix me. He didn't give me a speech about finding my true self. He said: 'You are a cool toy.' And then he said the thing that changed everything. He said: 'Over in that house is a kid who thinks you are the greatest. And it's not because you're a Space Ranger. It's because you're his.'"

Buzz let the words settle. Let them find their places.

Buzz: "I didn't become a Space Ranger again after that. I became something else. Something I didn't have a name for. I became a toy who chose to be a toy — not because I had to, not because the box told me to, but because being Andy's toy was worth more than being the universe's hero. Not lesser. Not a downgrade. Worth more. Because being needed by someone who loves you — being the thing they reach for when they're scared, the thing they hold when they sleep, the thing they whisper to when no one else is listening — that's not a consolation prize. That is the mission. The real one. The only one that ever mattered."

I remember every second of the fall. The air. The silence. The moment when the floor came up and my arm came off and the universe I believed in shattered like a snow globe dropped from a shelf. I remember thinking: this is it. This is the end of Buzz Lightyear.

But it wasn't the end. It was the beginning. Because the Buzz Lightyear who believed he could fly was a character on a box. The Buzz Lightyear who stayed — who chose to stay, who sat with a broken arm at a tea party and let a child pour him imaginary tea and found something worth being on the other side of that — he was real. He is real.

I am real. Not because I fly. Because I stay. — Buzz Lightyear
🚀 ⭐ 🚀

Across the room, Goku had stopped doing push-ups.

This was, by itself, notable. Goku had been doing push-ups almost continuously since the territorial dispute — on the carpet, as Woody had mandated, accepting the inferior surface with the stubborn grace of someone who believed that training was training regardless of conditions. He had done sets of fifty. Sets of a hundred. Sets that blurred together into an endless, repetitive rhythm that the other toys had learned to ignore the way you learn to ignore a ticking clock.

But now he was sitting. Cross-legged on the carpet near the blue crayon line, palms resting on his knees, eyes closed. His posture was perfect — spine straight, shoulders back, chin level — and if you didn't know better, you might have thought he was meditating.

He wasn't meditating.

He was trying to feel his ki.

It was the thing he hadn't told anyone. Not Naruto, who would have understood the loss of chakra. Not Luffy, who would have made a joke to fill the silence. Not even Tanjiro, who would have listened with those gentle eyes and offered comfort that Goku wasn't ready to accept. This was private. This was between Goku and the place inside himself where ki had always lived — the warm, golden core at the center of his being that had been there since before he could remember, since before Grandpa Gohan, since before the tournament, since before everything.

He reached for it the way he had ten thousand times before. Closed his eyes. Breathed in. Searched for the spark.

And found...

Nothing.

Not darkness. Not absence. Just... plastic. The smooth, uniform interior of a hollow torso. The click of joints. The faint chemical scent of new paint. Where his ki center should have been, there was a void — not a wound, not a scar, but a space that had never contained anything at all. Like reaching for a hand in the dark and finding that the person you were reaching for had never been standing there.

Goku opened his eyes.

Across the room, Woody was watching him. Not with suspicion or authority or the careful, measured concern of a leader monitoring a potential problem. Woody was watching with the look of someone who recognized what was happening because he had seen it before — in Buzz's eyes, years ago, at the top of a staircase in a neighbor's house.

Their eyes met. And Goku, for the first time since he'd punched his way out of an orange box, didn't smile. Didn't grin. Didn't reach for humor or optimism or the irrepressible cheerfulness that had carried him through death and resurrection and the end of the universe.

He just looked at Woody and gave the smallest nod. The kind of nod that said: I understand now. I didn't before, but I do now.

Woody nodded back.

Two leaders, across a blue crayon line, sharing a silence that held more than any speech either of them had ever given.

🔥 ⭐ 🔥

The afternoon softened.

Outside, clouds had drifted across the sun, and the light in Bonnie's room shifted from hard gold to something gentler — diffused, muted, the color of old photographs. The sprinkler had stopped. The house was quiet except for the distant hum of a television and the occasional burst of children's laughter from downstairs.

Tanjiro was the one who crossed the line.

Literally. He stepped over the blue crayon mark on the carpet — not aggressively, not defiantly, but the way someone steps over a crack in a sidewalk: acknowledging it, respecting it, but not letting it stop them. He walked to where Woody stood by the toy chest and bowed — the same bow he'd offered when he first emerged from his box, deep and sincere and rooted in a respect that transcended the circumstances.

Tanjiro: "Woody-san. May I speak with you?"

Woody looked at the Demon Slayer. At the checkered haori, the scar on his forehead, the painted eyes that somehow managed to convey a depth of feeling that the sculptor at the factory had almost certainly not intended.

Woody: "...Yeah. Yeah, go ahead."

Tanjiro: "You said something earlier. To Goku. You said: 'Ask me again after you've been loved by a kid.' I've been thinking about those words all afternoon. About what it means to be loved by someone you protect." He paused, choosing his next words with the care of someone for whom words were sacred. "In my world, I had a family. A mother. Brothers and sisters. They were... everything. The reason I breathed. The reason I trained. The reason I walked into darkness and faced things that should have killed me."

Woody: "What happened to them?"

The question was quiet. Not probing — Woody had enough instinct to know that some questions needed to be asked gently.

Tanjiro: "They were taken from me. By a demon. In one night, I went from a boy with a family to a boy with a mission. The only one who survived was my sister, Nezuko. And she was... changed. Transformed into the thing I was supposed to destroy." His voice carried no bitterness — only a grief so old and so carefully tended that it had become something almost beautiful, like a scar that had healed into a pattern. "I carried her. On my back. In a box. Through every fight, every mountain, every trial. Because she was my family. And family is what you carry, even when it hurts. Especially when it hurts."

Woody was very still. In thirty-plus years of being a toy, he had heard a lot of stories. Andy's whispered bedtime monologues. Bonnie's elaborate playdate epics. Jessie's story about Emily and the donation box — the one that still made his pull-string ache when he thought about it too long. He had heard stories of love and loss and the space between.

But Tanjiro's story had a weight that went beyond the telling. It wasn't just a narrative — it was a shape. The shape of a boy carrying his sister through hell because setting her down was never an option.

Woody: "You carried her. Even though she was the thing you were fighting against."

Tanjiro: "She was never the thing I was fighting against. She was the reason I was fighting at all."

A beat. Two.

Woody: "...I had a kid once. Andy. He was — he was everything Tanjiro. The way you talk about Nezuko — the way you carried her — that's how I felt about Andy. He was the reason. Not the missions, not the rescues, not the speeches. Andy. And when he grew up..." Woody's voice caught. A tiny hitch, almost mechanical, like a pull-string winding back. "He didn't need me anymore. And I had to learn that letting go wasn't the same as losing. That the love didn't go away just because the kid did."

Tanjiro: "Nezuko was cured. In the end. She became human again. And when I looked at her — standing in the sunlight, alive, whole, free — I understood that everything I had endured was worth it. Every scar. Every broken bone. Every night I thought I couldn't go on. Because she was standing in the light."

Woody: "Andy went to college. And the last thing he did — the very last thing — was play with me. One more time. In the front yard, with a girl named Bonnie who looked at me the way he used to. And he... he gave me to her." Pause. "He let me go so I could be needed again. And Bonnie — she's not Andy. She's different. She plays different. She loves different. But she loves. And that's the whole thing, Tanjiro. That's the entire job. Being there for the kid who needs you right now."

They stood together by the toy chest — a cowboy and a Demon Slayer, six inches tall, in a room full of butterfly curtains and glow-in-the-dark stars. And between them, something formed that was older and stronger than either of their worlds: the recognition of two people who had carried something precious through impossible circumstances and come out the other side still carrying.

Tanjiro: "Woody-san... I think I understand now. What you meant. About being loved."

Woody: "Yeah?"

Tanjiro: "It's not about being powerful. It never was. It's about being present. Being the thing someone reaches for. The thing they carry."

Woody: (very quietly) "...Yeah. That's it. That's exactly it."

In all my years as a toy, I've met a lot of figures. New toys, old toys, broken toys, forgotten toys. I've seen toys who defined themselves by their features — voice boxes and laser lights and pull-strings that played the same phrase a thousand times. And I've seen toys who defined themselves by something else entirely.

Tanjiro defined himself by who he carried.

And standing there, listening to him talk about his sister and the sunlight and the thing that made all the suffering worth it — I realized something I should have understood from the beginning. These six figures weren't lost warriors stumbling through a world they didn't fit. They were the same as us. They had just been carrying different things. — Woody
🌸 ⭐ 🌸

Night came to Bonnie's room the way it always did — gradually, then all at once.

The children had returned for one more round of play in the late afternoon — shorter this time, calmer, the kind of playing that happens when the sugar has worn off and the day is winding down. Bonnie had brushed Rex's teeth with her toothbrush ("He has CAVITIES, Dennis! Look at these TEETH!"). Dennis had staged a quiet scene where Tanjiro and Woody sat side by side on the windowsill, looking out at the sunset, and had told Bonnie: "They're thinking about stuff. Important stuff." Bonnie had nodded solemnly and placed Forky between them as "the counselor."

Then bedtime routines. Then the door closing. Then the hallway light clicking off one room at a time until the only light left was the moon through the curtains and the faint green glow of the star stickers on the ceiling.

Bonnie slept with Woody on one side and Jessie on the other, the way she always did — one hand on each, her small fingers curled around their familiar shapes. Dennis's figures were on the desk, lined up neatly. All six. Shoulder to shoulder.

In the dark, the room breathed.

And one by one, the anime figures woke.

Not woke — they hadn't been asleep. Toys didn't sleep. But there was a difference between existing in the stillness and choosing to inhabit it, and as the night deepened and the house settled into its own quiet rhythms, the six warriors — fighters, heroes, legends made of thermoplastic and painted memories — chose to be present in the dark.

Naruto was the first to speak, and for once, he spoke softly.

Naruto: "I miss ramen."

It wasn't a joke. It wasn't loud. It was the quiet admission of a man who had lost something so fundamental that the loss hadn't registered until the quiet was big enough to contain it.

Luffy: "I miss the sea."

Goku: "I miss flying."

Ichigo: "I miss my sisters."

Deku: "I miss All Might."

Tanjiro: "I miss the sound of Nezuko's breathing when she slept."

Six confessions. Six losses. Six figures sitting on a desk in the dark, shoulder to shoulder, separated from everything they had ever known by a distance that couldn't be measured in miles because it wasn't a distance at all — it was a category change. They hadn't traveled here. They had been manufactured here. And the things they missed — the ramen and the sea and the flying and the sisters and the mentor and the breathing — were memories of a world that existed only in the stories printed on the sides of their boxes.

And yet the missing was real. The grief was real. The love behind the grief was real. Whatever they were made of — plastic, paint, factory glue — the feelings inside them were as genuine as anything that had ever lived in any world.

Naruto: "Are we even... us? Or are we just... copies? Toys that think they're us?"

No one answered. Because no one knew. Because the question — the real question, the one hiding inside all the smaller questions — was too big for a desk in a dark room. It was the kind of question that didn't have an answer, only a choice.

Goku chose first.

Goku: "Does it matter?"

Naruto: "What?"

Goku: "Whether we're the originals or copies. Whether the memories are real or programmed. Whether we're warriors or toys. Does it matter?" He turned to look at them — five faces in the dark, five sets of painted eyes catching the moonlight. "Because right now, I'm sitting next to five people I'd fight for. And if I can't fight — if the powers are gone and the ki is gone and the Kamehameha is just a word — then I'll sit with you. That's what I've got. That's what I can do. And if that's all I can do..." The grin came back. Small. Real. Battle-tested. "...then I'll do it better than anyone has ever sat before."

Naruto: (snorting, but gently) "You can't be the best at sitting."

Goku: "Watch me."

It was a small moment. Ridiculous, even. A six-inch plastic figure declaring his intention to master the art of sitting, in a dark room that smelled faintly of crayons and fabric softener, on a desk that belonged to a child who would never know the conversation was happening.

But small moments, Woody had always known, were where the real stories lived.

The room settled. Bonnie turned in her sleep, pulling Jessie closer. Mr. Sprinkles padded past the closed door, his shadow sliding under the gap like a gray ghost. The glow-in-the-dark stars on the ceiling dimmed slowly — the way real stars did, he supposed, though none of them would ever see real stars from where they stood.

On the desk, six figures sat in the dark. Not warriors. Not legends. Not the shapes on their boxes. Just six souls who missed what they'd lost and hadn't yet found what they would gain — but were sitting together, which was enough. For now, it was enough.

And on the bed, nestled against a sleeping child's chest, a cowboy doll lay with his hat tipped forward and his painted eyes closed and his pull-string coiled neatly against his back. He wasn't asleep — toys didn't sleep — but he was still. Deeply, peacefully, completely still. The kind of still that only came from knowing exactly where you were supposed to be.

The line on the carpet was still there. Blue crayon, slightly smudged where Mr. Sprinkles had walked through it. Tomorrow it would still be there, and the day after that, and the day after that — until Bonnie's mom vacuumed or Bonnie herself noticed it and said "How did THAT get there?" and wiped it away.

But tonight — in the dark, in the quiet, in the space between what was and what would be — the line didn't mean what it had meant this morning. This morning it was a border. A wall. A separation between us and them.

Tonight it was just a mark on the floor. And on either side of it, in the soft glow of fading stars, eighteen toys were breathing the same air, feeling the same night, and carrying — each in their own way — the same impossible, beautiful, terrifying thing.

Hope.

Fragile, stubborn, unreasonable hope. The kind that doesn't make sense. The kind that doesn't need to. The kind that sits on a desk in the dark and decides, against all evidence and all logic and all the numbers on a green-haired boy's index card, that tomorrow might be different.

That's enough for now. That's enough for tonight.

Tomorrow, we'll see. — Woody
